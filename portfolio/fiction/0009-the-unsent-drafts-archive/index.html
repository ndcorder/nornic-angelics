Yuki,

Thank you for sending the data. I've reviewed the core sample anomalies from the drills and I want to be careful about what I say here because email is email and we both know how this works.

The depth markers at 140m don't correlate with anything in the expected record. Not orbital, not volcanic, not any known forcing. It's as if the ice sheet collapsed destabilized experienced a phase change at a rate that shouldn't be possible. The timescale is wrong by orders of magnitude.

I've run the numbers six times. I keep hoping I've made an error. I haven't made an error.

I think you see what I see. I think you've already run your own numbers. I think that's why you emailed me instead of posting to the group.

Can we talk? Not about this. About what we do with this. Because this will end careers if this is right, it changes the timeline for everything. Not in a century. In decades. Maybe less.

I need to hear your voice before I decide how to proceed.

—E

Maren,

You asked me at dinner why I look scared all the time. Your father said I was just tired. But you're sixteen now and you've always seen through me and I think you deserved a real answer, so here it is:

I'm scared because I understand something about the world that I can't un-understand. I'm scared because I know what's coming and I can't make anyone listen, and the people who should be listening are the ones who decide if I get to keep doing this work.

I'm scared because I look at you and I see how bright you are and how kind, and I think about the world you're going to live in, and I think: I helped break this. Not just by driving and flying and all the things everyone does, but by knowing. By knowing and not knowing how to make it stop.

Every glacier I study is a letter from a world that's dying. I read those letters for a living. And I go home and I make dinner and I try not to think about how there won't be any glaciers left by the time you're my age.

You asked if I was okay. I'm not okay. But I'm going to keep going because the alternative is to stop, and if I stop, then what was any of this for?

I love you more than the ice. I just don't know how to love you and do this work at the same time.

—Mom

To: yuki.tanaka@kyoto-u.ac.jp
Subject: None
Drafted: November 12, 2019, 1:22 AM  ·  Revisions: 31

Yuki,

Something happened to me today and I don't know how to describe it so I'm going to try and forgive me if this doesn't make sense.

I went for a walk after the session and ended up by the water and I stood there for a long time.

I realized tonight that I don't recognize myself anymore. I looked in the bathroom mirror at the hotel and I saw my mother's face, and my mother was a woman who spent her whole life being afraid, and I swore I would never become her.

But here's what I can't stop thinking: my mother was right to be afraid. She was right about everything. She was just wrong about what to do with the fear. She let it eat her. I let it make me work harder.

I have spent eleven years studying the death of ice. I have measured it, charted it, presented it, published it. I have stood in rooms full of people and told them the world is ending and they've nodded and refilled their wine glasses and asked about my methodology.

And I'm tired.

I don't mean I need a vacation. I mean I'm tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix. I mean there's a kind of exhaustion that comes from knowing something no one wants to know, and I've been carrying it alone for so long that I can't remember what it felt like to not know.

Do you remember that night in Svalbard? We sat outside the station and watched the sky and you said the aurora looked like the earth trying to speak. And I said no, it's the sun, it's always the sun, everything comes from the sun. And you laughed and said I couldn't just let something be beautiful.

You were right. I couldn't. I can't. I look at something beautiful and I immediately want to understand it, and the understanding is always a kind of loss.

I'm not going to do anything stupid. I want you to know that. I'm just telling you because I think you're the only person who I needed someone to know that I'm not okay.

I needed someone to know that I keep writing emails to my daughter and deleting them because every version sounds like a goodbye and I don't want it to be a goodbye.

I needed someone to know that I rewrote my findings so the Institute would keep funding me, and I can't tell if I was being strategic or cowardly, and the distinction feels important.

I needed someone to know that I can hear the ice in my sleep.

I needed someone to know my name.
