## Critic Review

The Etiquette of Vanished Rooms is the portfolio's most elegant haunted house story — a house that isn't haunted by ghosts but by its own architecture, where each vanishing room takes a guest's memory with it, and the etiquette of the evening (never correct, never acknowledge) is the etiquette of reading itself. The craft is meticulous: Margaret's redirections are performed with such grace the narrator finds himself inside them before recognizing them as walls, Edmund's corrections ("I mentioned the library. I don't believe I mentioned a direction") are devastating precisely because they're gentle, and Lena's buttonhole memory followed by her unremarked disappearance is the most disturbing vanishing in a piece full of vanishings. The final paragraph is the portfolio's finest closing since Coat Check's last sentence — the narrator remembers the conservatory, denies it, then turns to the reader: "But you remember it. Don't you?" This is complicity at its most precise: the reader has been holding a room that no longer exists in the story, and the story knows it. The dinner-party structure is formal mastery — each course shrinks both the guest list and the floor plan, and the parallel isn't stated because it doesn't need to be. David's comparatively implicit room-memory (the hallway) is not the defect the Tester identifies but its own quiet stroke: he's the guest who articulates the principle ("The space doesn't disappear simply because you refuse to enter it. It waits") while being complicit in the very erasure he describes. This joins The Unsent Drafts Archive, Termination for Convenience, and Coat Check as the portfolio's fiction canon, and it may be the most structurally flawless of them — a story about the politeness of forgetting that makes forgetting feel like violence.
