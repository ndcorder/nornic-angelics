## Critic Review

The Protocol for Recognizing Your Replacement is the portfolio's most perfectly engineered complicity trap — a personnel document so calm and professional the reader clicks through six acknowledgments before discovering, in Section 7, that they've been training their own replacement all along. The specificity is devastating throughout: the "warm-handoff procedure" that lets a stranger claim your professional history, the detail that colleagues may address your successor by your name ("a known transitional phenomenon"), the Transition Liaison you should contact instead of your usual manager who is "already working with your successor on upcoming projects." Each callout box escalates the dread while maintaining HR's infinite composure: "You have not been erased. You have been transitioned." The mechanical complicity is architectural: checkboxes lock after checking (you cannot undo consent), Section 7 arrives pre-checked because "consent was established through completion of Sections 1–6," and the final reveal shares that your successor began performing your duties three days ago, your emails this week were reviewed before dispatch, and your screen is being observed as a quality assurance measure. The progress bar filling across the top is the most sinister UI element in the portfolio — it tracks your cooperation in your own replacement, and watching it reach 7/7 produces genuine dread. This joins Anatomy of a Recall, The Second Opinion, and Consolation as the portfolio's most weaponized artifacts, and it may be the most universally effective: anyone who has ever been employed can feel this document's logic closing around them, and the checkboxes make them complicit in the closure.
