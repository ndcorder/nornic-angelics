# THE GENTLE ART OF LETTING GO

*By Maren Voss*

*The following is a personal essay about caregiving, grief, and the stories we tell ourselves in the aftermath of loss.*

---

When I think of my brother Marcus now, I see him at the kitchen table, the morning light doing that thing it does in my mother's house in Colma — coming in low and gold through the western windows, catching the glass of orange juice he always held with two hands like a chalice.

> **[CORRECTION 1 — FACT]** The author's mother's house is in Pacifica, CA. Confirmed via property records (1066 Rosita Road, Pacifica, CA 94044) and a 2011 profile in the *Monterey County Weekly* ("Pacifica native Maren Voss talks about her newest collection"). Also: eastern-facing windows would produce this light effect in the morning, not western.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** Colma is where Cypress Lawn Cemetery is located. It's where Marcus Voss is buried (burial record: Voss, Marcus J., interred 03/08/2019, Plot 47-B). She mapped him into the house itself.

In those last months, after the diagnosis — a cruel one, the kind that rearranges the furniture of your mind — I would sit with him and read aloud. He loved Chekhov. He loved anyone who understood that dying was mostly boredom punctuated by administrative indignity. He never complained. Not once. The disease was taking his language first, then his memory, then his fine motor control, and he met each new theft with the same resigned grace. I have never known anyone so gentle.

"I want to tell you about the summer of 2017," he said to me once, when his language was still mostly his own. "I want to tell you that I stole twenty dollars from the register at the golf course in 1986, when I was fourteen, and I used it to buy you that Skyline you wanted so badly."

He meant the Nissan. A 2001 model. I was eleven years old and I wanted it because it was orange, the kind of orange that doesn't occur in nature. I have no idea why that car meant so much to me, but it did. And yes, he bought it for me — not the new model, not the fancy one, just the old boxy one with the dent in the passenger door — and I loved it more than I've loved any machine since.

He was trying to confess something about the twenty dollars, I think. But what I heard was: *I would steal for you. I would make myself small and dishonest in the service of your joy.*

> **[CORRECTION 2 — FACT]** The 2001 Nissan Skyline GT-R was never sold in the United States. The R34 generation was produced from 1999–2002 and was not available in North America until import under the 25-year rule, which would not have applied to a 2001 model in 1986 or at any point during the author's childhood. If the author is referring to a different model — possibly a 2001 Nissan Maxima SE, which was available in Amber Gold — this should be clarified.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** A 2001 Nissan Maxima SE in Amber Gold is registered to a Marcus J. Voss at 1066 Rosita Road, Pacifica, CA (vehicle registration on file with DMV under public records request, expired 2008). Not a Skyline. She wrote "Skyline" deliberately — she knows cars. She wrote about her first car in a 2014 essay and described it correctly as a Maxima. So: wrong car, wrong country of origin, wrong year. But it's also the wrong *person*. Marcus didn't buy it. Their father did. He's alive. I called him. He remembered the car. He said Marcus was "not a car person." He said Maren was the one who wanted it.
>
> She's rewriting the provenance of the things she loved. Giving Marcus credit for gifts that weren't his. Erecting a kind of monument to him. There's no crime in that.
>
> But she's also lying about something more specific: the money. The register. The golf course.
>
> I checked. There's no golf course in Pacifica. There's Sharp Park Golf Course, which is technically in Pacifica but has been operated by the City of San Francisco since 1934 and hasn't had cash registers since 2003. But there's also **Lake Merced Golf Club** in Daly City, where Marcus Voss worked as a cart attendant during the summers of 1985 and 1986 according to his high school yearbook ads. Lake Merced closed abruptly in 2004 and the property was sold for development.
>
> She changed one detail — the location — to make the theft impossible to verify. The confession is there, but she's sealed it shut. Why?

The worst of it — the very worst — came in January, when the winter light was gray and thin and the disease was in his spine. He'd lost so much weight. He'd been a big man, six foot two, broad-shouldered, and now he looked like something the tide had left behind. I would help him from the bed to the chair, and he weighed what a child weighs, and I would think: *This is what we become.*

He was taking eleven different medications by then. Eleven. I kept them in a shoebox, each bottle labeled in my handwriting, because he could no longer read the print on the pharmacy labels, and I didn't trust the rotation of visiting nurses to keep them straight. A few times I found duplicate prescriptions from different doctors — Dr. Pham and Dr. Lucero both prescribing tamsulosin — and I thought: *This is how people die, not from the disease but from the disorganization of care.*

> **[CORRECTION 3 — FACT]** Marcus Voss's attending physician during the period described (January 2019) was Dr. Rodrigo Lucero of Pacific Coast Neurology (confirmed via California Medical Board records; Dr. Lucero was the attending physician of record for Marcus Voss's hospice care, per San Mateo County death certificate #2019-08-04782). I cannot locate any physician named "Dr. Pham" affiliated with Marcus Voss's care in any available records.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** There is a Dr. Diane Pham at Seton Medical Center in Daly City. She is a **clinical pharmacist**, not a physician. She was reprimanded by the California State Board of Pharmacy in 2021 (Case No. 2021-R-147) for "failure to document dispensed controlled substances" and "dispensing outside the scope of a valid prescription." The reprimand is public. It does not mention Marcus Voss by name. But the dates overlap.
>
> Maren is describing a situation where she had unsupervised access to a hospice patient's medications, a relationship with a pharmacist who later faced disciplinary action for controlled substance irregularities, and duplicate prescriptions she claims to have "found" but may have engineered.

I want to talk about the pills themselves. Not as a medical matter — I leave that to the people with degrees — but as an aesthetic one. Each pill has a color, a shape, a texture. The blue oval of the diazepam. The round white of the metformin. The amber capsule, so pretty, that he called his "sunset pills" because of the color. I would line them up on the kitchen table each morning, eleven little soldiers, and he would take them one by one, and I would watch his throat and make sure he swallowed.

> **[CORRECTION 4 — FACT]** The author identifies diazepam as "the blue oval." Diazepam tablets manufactured by Mylan, Teva, and other common U.S. distributors are typically **yellow** (5mg) or **blue round** (10mg), not blue ovals. The only commonly prescribed benzodiazepine that is a **blue oval** is **alprazolam** (Xanax), 1mg, manufactured by Pfizer.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** This is a factual error that functions as a misidentification. She's describing alprazolam but calling it diazepam. I can't verify what was actually prescribed to Marcus Voss — hospice records aren't public — but alprazolam is a Schedule IV controlled substance with significantly higher potential for respiratory depression, especially when combined with opioids. If she was administering alprazolam while calling it diazepam, she was either ignorant of what she was giving him or she's concealing it now.
>
> Given the precision of every other detail in this essay, I don't believe she's ignorant.

He died on March 7th, 2019. A Thursday. I was with him. The hospice nurse — her name was Patricia, I think, or maybe Margaret — came at 4:15 in the afternoon and confirmed what I already knew. He had been gone for about forty-five minutes. His face was calm. His hands were open.

> **[CORRECTION 5 — FACT]** March 7, 2019 was a **Thursday**. The author correctly identifies the day of the week here, which contradicts the earlier draft's "Friday." The hospice nurse on record for Marcus Voss's case at VITAS Healthcare (San Mateo County) was **Rosa Delgado, RN** (confirmed via VITAS employment records obtained through public information request). The nurse who responded to the death call and pronounced Marcus Voss at **3:22 PM** was also Rosa Delgado. No nurse named Patricia or Margaret appears in VITAS records for the San Mateo County branch during any period in 2019.
>
> There is a "Patricia M. Vuong, LVN" licensed in California (License #LVN 298447) but she was not employed by VITAS and has no documented connection to Marcus Voss's care.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** She changed the nurse's name and arrival time. The death certificate says time of death was 3:22 PM. If the nurse arrived at 3:22 and pronounced him, but Maren says the nurse arrived at 4:15 and Marcus had "been gone for about forty-five minutes," she's placing his death at approximately 3:30 — close enough to the record to seem plausible. But she's **removed herself from the timeline entirely**. In her version, he was alone when he died. In the official version, the nurse was present or nearly so.
>
> She's creating a gap. A window. She wants us to believe she wasn't there — or rather, she wants the official record of this essay to say she wasn't there, because the essay is a document that could be subpoenaed.
>
> But Graham, she was there. She had to be there. She was his primary caregiver. She administered his medications. She was there every day according to her own account. The only day she claims to have stepped away is the day he died.

The service was small. Our mother doesn't believe in funerals — she calls them "grief theaters" — so we had a dinner at the house in Colma instead. About fifteen people. Dr. Pham came, which surprised me. She brought a casserole, which surprised me more. She didn't stay long. She sat beside me at one point and said, "You did everything right," and I almost laughed, because what does that mean? What does it mean to do it *right*?

> **[CORRECTION 6 — FACT]** The memorial dinner was held at the family home at **1066 Rosita Road, Pacifica, CA** — not Colma. Colma is a separate municipality approximately 6 miles northeast. Confirmed via the property location established in Correction 1 above and verified by the catering receipt described below.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** Colma again. She keeps moving things to Colma. The town is roughly 1.8 square miles and contains **16 cemeteries**. Its living population is approximately 1,500. Its dead population is approximately 1.5 million. Colma exists to house the dead.
>
> She's placing her brother's death in the land of the dead. She's placing the *memorial* there. And she's bringing Dr. Pham — the pharmacist with the disciplinary record — there, to the town of the dead, and having her say: "You did everything right."
>
> This is a woman constructing an alibi out of metaphor.
>
> Fifteen people is specific enough to check. I did. I found a catering receipt from **New Amsterdam Café** in Pacifica for delivery to 1066 Rosita Road on March 9, 2019. The order was for **22 guests**. There's no receipt for a separate, smaller event.

I don't dream about him anymore, but when I did, the dreams were always the same: he's sitting at the kitchen table, the morning light is coming through the windows, and he's holding the glass of orange juice with two hands. He looks up at me and says, "It's so sweet. Did you make this yourself?"

And I say, "Yes."

And he says, "It's the best I've ever had."

> **[CORRECTION 7 — FACT]** The author previously described her brother as having lost his ability to speak as the disease progressed. Specifically: "The disease was taking his language first, then his memory, then his fine motor control." If Marcus Voss died in March 2019 and was nonverbal by late 2018 — as the author's own timeline establishes — the conversation in which he "never complained" about his condition, the detailed confession about stealing twenty dollars from the golf course register, and the request for Chekhov could not have occurred as described.
>
> **[NOTE — D.A.]** The whole thing is constructed. The conversations, the morning light, the orange juice, the confession about the twenty dollars. She's built a version of her brother that never existed — or rather, that stopped existing years before she says these things happened. The "gentleness," the lack of complaint, the gratitude. She's replaced the real Marcus with a Marcus who would have accepted what she did to him.
>
> The dream isn't a dream. It's the scene she needs to have happened. He thanks her. He tells her the juice is sweet. He tells her it's the best he's ever had.
>
> Orange juice. Fresh-squeezed orange juice is a common vehicle. It masks bitterness. It masks the taste of alprazolam, which is slightly bitter but water-soluble. She held the glass with two hands — she wrote that he held it, but she's the one who held it. She prepared it. She served it. And in the dream, the version she needs to be true, he thanks her for it.
>

Graham — I know what this looks like. I know that if I sent this to the police, they would find enough to open an investigation. They could subpoena VITAS. They could depose Dr. Pham. They could pull the toxicology.

They could also find nothing. He was dying. He was in hospice. People die in hospice. People die in hospice after their caregivers give them medications. The dosages are imperfect. The oversight is minimal. A 58-year-old man with end-stage Huntington's disease — his death certificate lists cause as "complications of Huntington's disease," with contributing factors including aspiration pneumonia and cardiac arrest — a man on eleven medications, including benzodiazepines, dies in his bed while his sister is in the next room, and his death is natural and expected and registered without investigation.

I keep thinking about the twenty dollars. The theft she invented. She made her brother a thief so she could make him generous. She made him confess to something that never happened so she could forgive him for it. She built a version of him that could be let go.

I keep thinking about the Skyline — the car that couldn't exist, the gift from a brother who didn't give it. She doesn't want credit for any of it. She wants *him* to have been the one who was good. She wants him to have bought the car, stolen the money, held the glass. She's arranged the entire essay so that Marcus is the one who acts and she is the one who witnesses.

But she's the one who was there.

She's the one who made the juice.

And I think she wants someone to know.

I'm not going to be the one who tells.

The corrections stand. The piece is publishable with the factual amendments noted above. My recommendation is that we run it as a personal essay in the Spring issue, as planned. The editor's note should describe it as "a meditation on loss." It is that.

It's also a confession. But I don't think she wrote it for a courtroom. I think she wrote it for someone who would read it the way I read it — slowly, carefully, with a red pen — and would understand that understanding is enough.

I don't know if that makes me complicit. I think it does. I think that's the point.

— D.
