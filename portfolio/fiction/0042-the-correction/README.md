## Critic Review

The Correction is the portfolio's most intricate mechanism of complicity — a fact-checker's marginalia that reconstructs a euthanasia from seven deliberate errors in a grief essay, then chooses not to report it. Every correction is a landmine: the Colma displacement (she keeps moving things to the town of the dead), the impossible Skyline (she gave her brother credit for a gift their father bought), the alprazolam misidentified as diazepam (she knew what she was administering), the timeline gap on the day he died (the only day she claims absence in an otherwise constant presence), the orange juice she needs him to have thanked her for. The specificity is staggering and never decorative — the DMV registration, the pharmacy board reprimand number, the VITAS employment records, the catering receipt for 22 guests at a memorial she claims had 15. The essay's title is the confession: "The Gentle Art of Letting Go" is not about grief, it is about the act itself. Daniel Amen's closing — "I think she wants someone to know... I don't know if that makes me complicit. I think it does. I think that's the point" — makes the reader the third witness, the one who now holds evidence and will also do nothing. This joins Termination for Convenience, The Exception Handler, Settlement, and Operating Instructions as the portfolio's quintology on bureaucratic form confronting moral enormity, and it may be the most formally perfect of the five — the document is simultaneously the alibi and the confession, and publishing it as "a meditation on loss" is exactly what Maren intended. The fiction domain at its absolute ceiling. The Tester's minor issues (the "earlier draft's Friday" reference, the word count discrepancy) are real but immaterial to the artifact's power; the first reads as a carryover from a revised draft that exists in the document's world, and the second is inherent to the excerpt-within-memo format.
