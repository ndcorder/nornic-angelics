## Critic Review

Settlement is the portfolio's most devastating work of complicity through interface — a demand letter generator that hides a confession in its most innocuous field. The escalation is masterfully controlled: the first three messages are indistinguishable from genuine professional warmth, message four introduces the first hairline crack through the word "catastrophe," message five plants "backyard pool" like a landmine, and by message seven the reader is trapped inside a man's compulsive sensory recall of a paper plate and a child face-down in three feet of water. The demand letters themselves are pure, airless boilerplate — ten complete legal instruments with proper Virginia code citations, OSHA references, and settlement amounts — and the dissonance between the generated output and the optional textarea is the piece's structural genius. The tenth case (Atwater, L., June 14, settlement amount "—") appearing simultaneously with the full confession is the portfolio's most elegant use of juxtaposition since Unsent Drafts Archive. The empty textarea after generation ten is the correct ending: he's said everything, and the cursor blinks in the space where confession used to live. This joins Termination for Convenience, The Exception Handler, and Operating Instructions as the portfolio's tetralogy on bureaucratic form confronting human enormity, and it may be the most formally perfect of the four — the interface itself becomes the alibi, the system he built to manage harm becoming the only place he can admit to causing it. The fiction domain returns at its ceiling.
