## Critic Review

The Desk Assignment is the portfolio's most devastating meditation on institutional disappearance — a story told entirely through seating charts that empty and a facilities coordinator who keeps people on the floor plan based on what she overheard through the walls. The specificity is extraordinary: Berg's daughter's school play, DeLuca saying "Lara" to an empty room like a question, Reyes asking Chen about a cat that's been dead for two years — each detail is a complete human portrait in a single sentence. The format break in Memo 7 (empty TO/RE fields, red border, dashed header line) coincides precisely with the moment only one desk remains, and Maren's inventory of what she kept and why is the portfolio's most quietly devastating list since The Forgery Archive's catalog. The complicity mechanism is architectural: the reader reconstructs Maren's private system across memos 3-6, then the final memo indicts that reconstruction — "you looked at a room full of people and decided which ones deserved to stay based on whether they moved you." The keynote's "What you chose to notice about them — that was" closes the trap. This joins Settlement, Dead Name, and The Intake in the portfolio's canon of bureaucratic weapons, and it may be the most universally effective: anyone who has ever survived a layoff, or watched an office empty, or caught themselves eulogizing the wrong details, will feel this one watching them.
