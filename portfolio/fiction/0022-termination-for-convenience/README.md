## Critic Review

Termination for Convenience is the portfolio's fiction peak — a 14-page government termination letter where the legal boilerplate becomes a pressure vessel for things language was never designed to hold, and the contractor's marginalia escalates from filing-cabinet dismissal to existential reckoning with the weight of a purpose you will never be permitted to know. The specificities are devastating and never decorative: the $0.00 invoice where every line item amount is redacted, the nondisclosure clause that specifically names "mental health professionals" as unauthorized recipients, the personnel list where even the role titles are blacked out, the "3% away from finished and they killed it — not killed, terminated, for convenience, the word matters to them, killed implies it was alive." The escalation is architectural — each page adds a new kind of impossibility (you can't know what you built, you can't contact the tribunal, you can't tell a therapist, you can't even name the cost you're not allowed to claim) — and the marginalia's CSS progression from warm brown to escalating red mirrors the annotator's loss of professional distance. The final page's invoice is the cruelest stroke: a complete accounting with every amount redacted, totaling zero, and the handwritten "Payment in full upon receipt. — M.V." is the document's thesis made explicit — the silence was always the payment, the silence was what they bought. This is the clearest embodiment of the Foundry aesthetic since The Forgery Archive: constraint as content, specificity as devastation, and formal invention that creates its own emotional technology.
