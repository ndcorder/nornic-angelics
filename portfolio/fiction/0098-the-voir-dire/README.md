      A questionnaire that begins as a legal voir dire — procedural questions
      about your ability to be impartial, your relationship to the parties — but
      the case is never stated. Each question narrows. "Is there someone you
      think about more than they think about you?" "Have you ever kept something

      the charges are unwritten, and their answers have already convicted them.

## Critic Review

The Voir Dire is the portfolio's most formally disciplined complicity trap — a twelve-question jury questionnaire that begins as bureaucratic procedure and becomes, question by question, a trial where you are both defendant and sole witness, and the charges are never stated because you already know what they are. The escalation is masterfully paced: Q1-4 are genuine voir dire questions (name, prior service, relationship to parties, impartiality), Q5 ("Have you ever been called to account for something you did — or didn't do — and felt the judgment was unfair?") is the pivot disguised as procedure, and Q6-8 ("Is there someone you think about more than they think about you?"; "When was the last time you kept something that wasn't yours to keep?"; the checkbox admissions including "Waited for someone who was not coming") are devastating because they remain formally identical to the legal questions that preceded them. The echo mechanic in Q10 — which inserts your Q6 and Q7 answers back into the question as italicized evidence — is the moment of full recognition: you provided the testimony that convicts you. Q11's "off the record" promise is the cruelest lie in the portfolio, because Q12 quotes it back at you before asking "do you believe you are innocent?" and then dissolves every character you try to type. The silence that replaces your answer IS the verdict. The end sequence — "Questionnaire Complete," then "Thank you for your service. You are excused.," then nothing but a blinking cursor — is perfect: the court doesn't need to render judgment because your answers already did. This joins The Confession Booth and Exfiltration as the portfolio's most weaponized interactive work, and may be the most devastating: where those artifacts asked you to act, this one only asks you to answer honestly.
