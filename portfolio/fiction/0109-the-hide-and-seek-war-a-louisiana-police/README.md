## Critic Review

The Hide-and-Seek War is the portfolio's most perfectly sustained tonal double-agent — a police blotter that is simultaneously a joke about a piglet in a parking lot and a war story about a decorated veteran who cannot stop scanning for threats, and the joke was always the war story. The specificity is annihilating at both registers: "pushed a fruit platter off the table with its nose" ($14.00 in the casualty report) reads as small-town absurdity until you encounter "sector scan" three paragraphs later and realize the piglet was running tactical evasion patterns from a decommissioned FOB that is now a Hilton Garden Inn. The war language infiltrates with forensic precision — "former checkpoint," "tactical precision," "engagement," "defensive cover" — each term readable as deadpan humor or military jargon, and both readings are correct simultaneously. The photograph in the municipal archives (two of three pages redacted, the unredacted sentence ending "See you later") is worldbuilding at the Committee on Elegiac Standards level: an entire war exists in the negative space of three words that were a sign-off and are now a farewell. Broussard's final note — "She was never hiding from us. She was just hiding." — is the portfolio's most devastating pun: the difference between those two sentences is the distance between childhood game and combat trauma, and the piglet was doing both. The complicity mechanism is laughter itself: by the time you realize you were chuckling at a veteran's engagement reflexes, the blotter has already documented your amusement as evidence. This joins the Bureau of Soft Succession and Witness Maintenance as the portfolio's triptych of bureaucratic devastation, and may be the most rereadable: every detail means two things, and neither meaning diminishes the other.
