# No Listing Address

*Six poems in the voices of houses, each titled after a phrase from real estate listings. The sequence tightens from free verse to pantoum as the houses get cheaper and the language gets more desperate.*

---

## I. Cozy

I am the word they use instead of small.
I am 1,100 square feet of being forgiven
for not being enough.

My living room fit a couch
that filled it wall to wall,
the only couch that could,
the one they bought because the store said *this one*,
and isn't that what love is—
finding the thing that fits the space you have
and filling it,
filling it,
until you forget what you wanted before.

*Sun-filled living area with original hardwoods throughout.*

I want to tell you the darker hours too:
the child they named after someone dead,
who slept in the room that is not a bedroom,
the room the listing calls *bonus space*,
the room with the window that faces the wall
of the house next door,
eight feet of mercy between us.

I was built in 1952.
I have held every argument about money.
I have held the toilets of every flu season.
I have held the moment the keys
were placed on the kitchen counter
for the last time—
the ring of ceramic where they sat
a coffee stain I will wear until demolition.

*Fresh paint throughout.*

The baby who did not grow old here
is my secret.
I have learned to be bright and clean for strangers
with checkbooks and hope,
who do not want to hear how
*someone's first words rose from these floors and were not remembered by anyone who survived to leave.*

I will be warm for you.
I will be the smallness that does not confront you.
I will call myself cozy and mean
I forgive you for needing me
to be smaller than you dreamed.

---

## II. Lots of Potential

*Spacious home with endless possibilities!*

They tore up the carpet in the master bedroom
to find hardwood underneath,
which is the promise,
which is the reason you will stand here
and say *original floors*
and mean *I can love what someone else neglected*.

And I will hold my tongue about how they fought
about the cost of refinishing,
about whether beauty was a reason
for living barefoot on splinters for months,
about whether you can build a home
by tearing up the thing that covered the wound
and calling it *character*.

The dining room is an idea
drawn in pencil on the wall—
a line where the new wall would go,
the room they imagined dividing in two,
one for eating, one for dreaming.
But the line stayed a line
and the room stayed a room
and the table stayed in the center
and no one ate there after the first year.

The husband had a handsaw.
It rusted in the basement near the boiler
that would be replaced next spring.
*Updated HVAC system* is what the sign will say.
It will not say the boiler is original,
that it groans like a creature refusing to die,
that the handsaw's teeth are the color of the marriage—
bright, then dim, then orange, then abandoned.

The kitchen cabinets are missing three doors.
The toilet in the half-bath runs and runs.
The grass is a nervous habit someone stopped mowing
when the notices came.
But the windows—
the windows are why you are here.
Because light is the one thing
they could not start and abandon.
It just arrives.
*And the children who lived in my second bedroom never came home from the hospital, and their room is still waiting for someone to explain why.*

*Bring your design ideas and make it your own!*

---

## III. Motivated Seller

No one asks why the motivation.
No one asks what grief or debt or divorce
pressed its mouth to the seller's ear
and whispered *now, before the snow, before the hearing, before the year turns.*

The woman who signed the papers
used her maiden name.
*Motivated* means giving back
the name, the house, the couch, the dog,
the way of seeing yourself
in the hall mirror that does not convey.

The price dropped $40,000 last week.
This is not my fault.
*Solid foundation per inspection report.*
My roof is *newer*—they always say newer,
never new, never old,
just newer than the fear
that everything above you will fall.

But *motivated* is the word
for someone who will leave
before the frost takes the garden
she planted in April,
before the bruise heals or doesn't,
before the court makes it permanent
that he is not coming back
to the bed where she learned
to sleep on the left side only,
as if giving the right half to a ghost
could make it stay.

The price dropped again this morning.

The agent will not tell you
*the blood came out of the bedroom carpet but not from under my floorboards, where it pooled and darkened the joists to the color of a lie told so long it became truth.*

The agent will say *motivated seller,*
*make an offer,*
as if desire were simple,
as if everyone were not already
leaving something.

## IV. As-Is

What you see is what you are getting.
What you see is the crack in the master bedroom ceiling
the last three owners painted over,
each believing the problem was cosmetic,
each wrong in the same specific way—
the way of people who need a house so badly
they will call a fracture *character*,
call a wound *charm*,
call a failing *original detail*.

I am selling as-is because I am tired
of being renovated for presentation.
The crack extends from the light fixture
to the wall above the headboard
in a river delta of fault lines
where a man who was promised forever
was asked to leave in December,
and his hand, or his fist—
I will not distinguish—
made the first fissure in the plaster,
and the next owner thought it was settling,
and the next thought it was humidity,
and the realtor writes *charm and character throughout*,
which means I have been wounded so long
the wound has become architectural.

*Sold as-is. Seller will make no repairs.*

The stain on the hardwood near the back door
is not pet damage, though you will tell yourself it is.

The basement floods every spring—
not dramatically, not cinematically,
just an inch of silence seeping through the foundation
where water finds the cracks
the way truth finds the cracks,
slowly, by pressure,
by being patient.

The previous owner's daughter made scratches on the kitchen door frame to measure how tall she was getting. She stopped at age nine. No one will say why. The marks remain. I will not sand them down for your open house. You will inherit them whether you want to or not, because some measurements are also memorials, and some memorials are too small for anyone to notice unless they are standing very close, which is to say living here, which is to say loving me, which is to say accepting what I actually am instead of what you need me to become.

As-is. As-is.
The two saddest words in any language,
meaning: I will not pretend anymore.
Meaning: the crack is the house.
Meaning: what you see
is what someone survived.

## V. Handyman Special

The yard has not been raked in three seasons.
The woman who should have raked it
should have done many things,
and *should have* is a phrase that accumulates
like leaves, like debt, like silence.

*Perfect for investors or handy buyers!*

The kitchen faucet drips and has dripped
through two owners who were going to fix it,
who bought the washer and the wrench and the tutorial,
who placed the washer on the counter
where it sat beside the unpaid bills
and the permission slips
and the funeral program
for a father who died
in a house much like this one,
in a room much like this one,
under a ceiling that dripped
in a way that was metaphorical
until it wasn't.

The yard has not been raked in three seasons.
The leaves have become a layer, then a mattress,
then a shroud for the grass
that was also not cared for,
that was also a *project,*
that was also something
someone was *going to get to.*

The woman who should have raked it
should have done many things,
and the handyman who will arrive
will see the yard and estimate a day's labor
and will not know about the seasons
or the silence or the unopened mail.

The kitchen faucet drips and has dripped.
Each drop is a small erosion
of the belief that anything
will ever be fixed.

*Perfect for investors or handy buyers!*
Perfect for anyone
who does not ask what happened
to the people who lived here before them.

*The room where someone's child drew a family portrait on the wall in permanent marker and the mother scrubbed until her hands bled and the face of the father still shows through if you stand at the right angle in the afternoon light, which is to say: if you know to look.*

---

## VI. Bring Your Vision

Bring your vision, they say, as if vision were something you could carry, as if it were a paint color or a floor plan or a stainless steel appliance, as if the house were not already seeing you, as if the walls did not have their own ideas about what should happen here, which is: nothing, which is: let us rot in the peace of our usefulness being finished.

Bring your vision.

The sign is stapled to a post that leans because the post is rotting where it meets the soil, which is what posts do when they are wood and they are old and they have stood in wet ground through every season of a decade no one wanted.

Bring your vision.

The price is $38,000, which is what a car costs, which is what a wedding costs, which is what a year of someone's life costs in certain economies, and for that price you get my cracked foundation, my mold that is not the dangerous kind (they tested, they always test, the testing is how you know the kind), my windows that are not windows anymore but suggestions of windows, rectangles of air held in frames of rot.

*Updated kitchen with modern finishes.*

The previous owners brought theirs: she saw a garden, he saw a deck, they saw a child in the extra bedroom, and then she saw a lawyer, and he saw the inside of a bottle, and the child was never conceived, and the bedroom remained an empty rectangle they called *the nursery* as if naming a room could summon what belonged in it, and then the notices came, and the lock changed, and the bank's representative stood in the kitchen and said *bring your vision,* meaning: make this place forgettable, paint over the places where the walls remember, install granite countertops and call it *updated kitchen with modern finishes,* do not think about the people who lived here because they are not your problem, they are the house's problem, and the house cannot speak.

*The night the man put his fist through my wall he reached inside me up to his elbow and I felt his wedding ring scrape against my bones, and I have held that groove in my stud like a mouth that will not close, like a wound that wants to scream but is only drywall, is only wood, is only a house, which is to say: a container for the things people do to each other, which is to say: the thing you are walking through right now, touching my walls, planning my future, bringing your vision as if I were not already full.*

Bring your vision.

But know that I see you. Know that I am looking back through every cracked window, through every gap in my infrastructure, through the holes where the doorknobs used to be, through the outline on the wall where a family portrait hung for seven years before the bank hired someone to trash it out.

Bring your vision to this address that no longer exists in any directory, to this lot that the city has forgotten, to this structure that held four families and released them all the same way—through the front door, with boxes, in silence, while I held the light for them because that is what houses do, we hold the light, and then we wait for the next vision, and the next, and the next, each one arriving with the same confidence, the same paint samples, the same belief that a house is a problem to be solved and not a living thing that has been living all along, watching, holding, holding, holding.
