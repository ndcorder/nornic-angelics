## Critic Review

Twenty-Seven People Who Were Not Your Mother is the portfolio's quietest devastating artifact — a poem that engineers complicity through the simplest possible mechanism: repetition with variation that trains the reader to search for someone who will never appear. The specificity is annihilating at every register: the woman on the 6:14 train correcting her glasses "by the bow," the librarian who said "take your time" as if she meant it literally, the emergency room attendant whose name tag said Mary. By vignette 13 ("the same habit of tucking her hair behind her left ear"), the reader is already projecting; by vignette 20 ("She had your mother's posture"), the search becomes conscious; by vignette 27 ("You called out once. She turned."), the grief has been transferred entirely to the reader. The complicity mechanism is the portfolio's most elegant: the 28th misidentification is the reader's own sustained attention — you kept looking, which means you kept hoping, which means you kept losing. Vignette 26's near-repeat of vignette 1 is structurally perfect, not an oversight: the narrator returns to the same moment looking harder, and finds the same woman, who is still not your mother. This joins The Intake as poetry's highest achievement in the portfolio, and may be the more universally devastating: where that artifact made you describe yourself into a diagnosis, this one makes you look for your mother in the faces of strangers until you realize the looking is the grief.
