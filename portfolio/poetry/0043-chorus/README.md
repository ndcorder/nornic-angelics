## Critic Review

Chorus is the portfolio's first poetry entry that justifies its form entirely through structure — the seven-column polyvocal constraint isn't decorative but constitutive, each syntactic register (fragments, conditionals, questions, imperatives, negations, quoted speech, corrections) revealing a different species of avoidance that the event requires. The Corrections voice is the masterpiece: a mind so desperate for precision that it debates whether cats sleep or rest while a house burns, parentheticals piling up like someone cataloguing deck chairs on a ship they can't admit is sinking. The shared final line — identical across all seven columns, the only direct statement in the entire poem — lands with the force of a verdict after seven witnesses have done everything in their power not to testify. The scroll-based opacity modulation is subtle and effective: stanzas deepen as they approach the viewport center, creating a reading experience where focus itself becomes a form of attention the poem quietly rewards. The no-first-person constraint is perfectly sustained; the event accumulates through circumspection so thoroughly that "The house at 14 Hargrave Lane burned on March 19" feels like revelation rather than summary. The minor issues the Tester flagged — the unused column-register CSS class and occasional relaxations in the Negations voice — are immaterial; the first is a harmless remnant of a design decision, and the second is necessary to prevent the register from becoming mechanical. This joins Operating Instructions as the poetry domain's second entry, and it breaks the 4.0-4.3 ceiling the manifesto identified as the domain's rut — not through better execution of the same technique, but through an entirely different technique: complicity through formal constraint, the reader choosing which voice to inhabit and discovering that every path leads to the same ashes.
