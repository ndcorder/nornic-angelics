# Witness Marks

*Seven poems. Seven joints. Each poem's form dictated by its joinery — the architecture of connection progressing from complex interlocking toward pure surface contact, from craft toward will.*

---

## I. SCARF

She measures twice across the years
and finds the seam she feared was lost—
a lapping grain, a shared estate,
two timbers yielding into one.

Two timbers yielding into one,
where one had been a length of shadow,
where shadow learned to hold its shape
against the pressure of the join.

Against the pressure of the join
she finds the lap she feared was lost,
a measured length, a shared estate,
she measures twice across the years.

*The scarf joins two members end to end through overlapping. The second stanza begins where the first ends; the third begins where the second ends; the fourth returns to the opening — grain traveling both directions at once.*

---

## II. DOVETAIL

I cut the tails first—wedge-wide, proud—
you mark the pins from where I pressed,
the splay of holding, slight beyond
the negative, the spaces left,
so when I push the grain seats home,
so when you push I take you in,
resistance that resists by shape,
the fit that holds by refusal.
No glue. No nail. No iron claim.
Only the locked geometry
of wanting in and being kept.

*The dovetail joint interlocks through complementary geometry — protrusions fitted into recesses, the shape itself preventing withdrawal. Two voices alternate, each line the precise negative of its counterpart, until the final line: the seated joint, two voices holding the same position.*

---

## III. MORTISE AND TENON

I hollowed out a place for you—
not from abundance but from need,
not from the plenty of the grain
but from the wound of it, the hollow
I made by removing what was sound.

You came already shaped to fit.
Already whittled to the task.
Already narrowed to the point
of entering—shoulders dressed,
cheeks prepared, the whole of you
designed for disappearing into.

This is the gravity of joinery:
that something must be emptied
so something else can enter.
That the emptiness is not the cost
but the condition. That the tenon
does not apologize for its readiness.
That the mortise does not mourn
the wood it gave up. That they meet
in the dark. That the dark is enough.

*The mortise-and-tenon: a cavity and a protrusion, one designed for the other. The first stanza creates absence, the second creates presence, and the third witnesses their meeting in darkness.*

---

## IV. LAP

Not one inside the other.
Not carved hollow and filled.
Not interlocking teeth or hidden wedges.

Just each of us
relieved of half our depth—
flat where we were round,
open where we were whole—

so when we lie together
we make a surface
flush.

The scar of the saw
facing the scar of the saw.

*The lap joint joins two members by removing half the thickness of each. Neither penetrates. Neither envelopes. The poem mirrors this: two surfaces meeting through equal loss.*

---

## V. BRIDLE

I am slit to receive you.

Not cut away to nothing—
not the slow surrender of the lap—
but opened. Kept intact
on either side.
A channel.
A running Yes.

And you: narrowed at the end.
Just the end.
Still full where full is needed.
Diminished only
where diminishment
allows approach.

We call it bridle.
As if restraint.
As if the mouth
were gentled.

But see how the tongue
slips in.
See how the prongs
embrace it.

See how simple
the admission.

*The bridle joint: a slot cut through one member, a corresponding tenon on the other that passes entirely through. The poem moves through opening, narrowing, insertion — the tenon's end remains visible, proof of connection.*

---

## VI. MITER

What you notice is the corner.
What you notice is the angle
resolved to ninety degrees
without visible join,
without pin or spline
or overlap announcing itself.

Two edges
angled at forty-five.
Meeting.

The seam is a line you see
only if you know to see it.
Only if you press your thumb
across the grain and feel
the faintest ridge where two worlds
touch.

Hidden logic at the interface.
The joint is theoretical—
held by friction and by faith—
until the glue dries.
Until the chemical bond replaces
the mechanical one.

The weakest joint.
Almost nothing.
A shared edge.
A mutual belief in angles.

Beautiful
because it refuses
to show its work.

*The miter: two members cut at complementary angles, end grain hidden at the seam. The connecting logic kept just out of sight.*

---

## VII.

Surface pressed to surface.

No interlocking.
No carved receptacle.
No pinned through-tenon.
No splayed dovetail refusing release.

Just end grain pressed to face.
The porous end
against the stubborn face.

Nothing holds us
but pressure.
Nothing holds us
but the continuing
decision
to press.
