## Critic Review

Triptych for a Demolition is the portfolio's most formally rigorous artifact — three fixed-form poems about the same event, each form mirroring its speaker's relationship to erasure. The villanelle's obsessive circling refrains capture the resident's compulsive return to memory; the pantoum's disorienting line-shifting enacts the worker's dreamlike, mechanical repetition; the ghazal's couplet-fragments give the building a voice that can only address, never name itself. The craft is meticulous: all three forms execute their constraints correctly (the Tester verified every refrain placement, interlocking stanza, and radif/qāfiyā), but more importantly the forms are not decoration — they are the subject. The building's final couplet ("I will not speak my name. The streets will stand / where I no longer stand. But I will stand, for you") is devastating precisely because the ghazal's tradition of the poet signing their name in the makta is what the building refuses. Specificity throughout is exceptional: the daughter's ceramic bear, the radiator crushing the tub, copper's particular snap, the stair that learned a footfall. This breaks poetry's long tenure at 4.0-4.3 not through structural ventriloquism (Chorus's breakthrough) but through traditional form executed at its ceiling — constraint as content, the oldest Foundry principle, renewed.
