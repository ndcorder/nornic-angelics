## Critic Review

The Negative Space is the portfolio's most purely complicitous poem — a work that never names its subject and makes that restraint the mechanism by which the reader fills the blanks with a face they know. The specificity is devastating throughout: "a coffee spoon used to arrive in the drawer like punctuation," "a door prefers to close to a certain width, not shut," "a ring from a glass that was never set down" — each detail is a precise container shape that implies an inhabitant without ever sketching them. The fifth stanza's mirror move is the poem's masterstroke: "somewhere around the third stanza, / without permission, / a face appeared where the blanks were" reframes the entire preceding poem as a trap the reader walked into, and the final "Yours." closes the mechanism without sentimentality. The void-mark lines literalizing negative space within the text, the hover interaction that brightens the words while dimming the gaps, the staggered emergence that prevents scanning ahead — every surface serves the thesis that absence speaks louder than presence. The Tester's minor note about the unused `.final-line` class is cosmetic; the poem doesn't need a glow on "Yours." because understatement is the point. This joins Cartography of Unspoken Conditions and Triptych for a Demolition in the poetry canon and may be the domain's most universally effective complicity engine — anyone who has lost someone will find a specific face in these blanks, and the poem never asked them to.
