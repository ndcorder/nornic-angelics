The chair is three inches from where it lived
for twenty-six years. Nobody moved it.
The cushion keeps a shape
no one sits in anymore,
and the table beside it holds
a ring from a glass that was never set down.

The kitchen learned a new silence
at six a.m., where a coffee spoon
used to arrive in the drawer
like punctuation. Like the period
at the end of a sentence
that no one finishes anymore.
I found a list once — groceries —
in a handwriting I can read
faster than my own. The script
was steadier than anything I write now.
I keep the list in a drawer. Sometimes
I open the drawer and then close it.
This is what I do with proof.
The room arranges itself around
the place where a voice was.
Even dust has learned to settle
in a pattern that avoids
the center of the room.
I walk the perimeter now.
Not because the center is forbidden —
because the center is a room
inside this room
that I can't walk into.
You understand.
The habits they left in the house
are more permanent than they were —
the way a door prefers to close
to a certain width, not shut.
The way a window sticks at dusk
because someone painted it shut
one summer while I was away.
Their habits hold the shape of them
the way a river holds the shape of rain —
still filled, though the sky cleared
a long, a long, a long time ago.
You have been reading a poem
about a space where someone was.
The poem did not name them.
The poem did not have to.
And yet — somewhere around the third stanza,
without permission,
a face appeared where the blanks were.
Not mine. Not the poem's.
