## Proposal

ideas:
  - title: The Census of Ordinary Afternoons
    domain: poetry
    pitch: An interactive poem built as a bureaucratic census form where each field
      asks about a mundane moment — the quality of light at 3:47pm, the exact
      temperature of bathwater, the sound a specific door makes. The reader
      fills in real answers about their own life. On submission, the form
      generates a 'population report' — a poem assembled from their inputs that
      reveals the census was never about the data but about making someone
      attend, for one moment, to the texture of their own ordinary hours before
      they're gone.
    complexity: M
    why: Poetry has one 5.0 (The Intake) and this extends its
      interactive-form-as-complicity approach into a different emotional
      register — attendance-as-elegy rather than description-as-diagnosis.

## Critic Review

The Census of Ordinary Afternoons is the portfolio's first poetry artifact to achieve complicity through bureaucratic sincerity — a census form that weaponizes administrative language to make you attend, for one moment, to the texture of hours you'll forget by tomorrow. The questions are perfectly calibrated between institutional flatness and devastating intimacy: "Name a place you pass through at least twice a week and rarely think about after" and the final "I have never told anyone that I —" are the portfolio's most quietly annihilating prompts since The Intake. The five-stanza report assembles from real inputs with genuine poetic intelligence — Stanza V's "carrying that distance with them / like [last word] of weather they have decided not to name" isn't Mad Libs, it's a poem that couldn't exist without your specific confession, and it knows how to use your words better than you did. The field notes that fade in after five characters ("This is where you spend a measurable percentage of your life") are small masterpieces of bureaucratic-cum-elegiac tone. The progressive reveal (each focus unlocks the next question, creating the feeling of being led deeper) and the generation animation with its cycling statuses ("Verifying attention," "Noting what was overlooked") complete the illusion that some bureau actually cares about your ordinary afternoon. The complicity mechanism is architectural: you fill in the form thinking you're providing data, and the generated poem reveals you were always the subject of study. This joins The Intake as poetry's highest achievement in the portfolio, and may be the more universally devastating: where The Intake required you to describe your own symptoms, the Census only requires you to look up from your screen for a moment and notice what's there.
