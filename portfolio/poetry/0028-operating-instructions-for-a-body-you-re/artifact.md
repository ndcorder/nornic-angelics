## SECTION 2: DRAINING THE EMOTIONAL RESERVOIRS

**Overview:** The emotional reservoirs (see Fig. 2a, internal) must be drained before extraction can proceed. A fully loaded emotional system adds significant weight and may snag during removal. Many vacators attempt to leave with reservoirs intact. All regret it.

### 2.1 Locating the Reservoirs

The primary reservoir sits behind the sternum. You will know it by how certain songs and weather patterns affect it. This is the grief reservoir. It is the largest and most stubborn. Begin here.

Secondary reservoirs are distributed:
- Lower abdomen: anxiety
- Throat: unspoken sentiments
- Palms: the desire to reach for another
- The backs of the knees: sudden trust in strangers

### 2.2 Draining Procedure

Grief will not drain on command. It must be tricked. The following methods have shown a 94% success rate:

1. Visit a place you promised to visit together. Go alone.
2. Hold an object that retains warmth longer than it should.
3. Stand at a window during the hour you used to call someone.

**Important:** Do not drain directly onto others. Contamination risk is high. Others may interpret your drainage as an invitation to fill you again. This is how reservoirs refill.

**Note:** The reservoir in the sternum contains a sediment layer. This is normal. The sediment is composed of accumulated instances of tenderness that settled because you failed to circulate them when given the chance. It is too late to circulate them now. The next occupant may find it useful.

### 2.3 Confirmation of Complete Drainage

Drainage is complete when:
- You hear a song and do not imagine playing it for someone.
- You see a sunset and your first thought is "the light is scattering through atmosphere at a low angle" and nothing more.
- You can say "I love you" and hear only a measurement of distance.

Do not proceed to Section 3 until all criteria are met.

## SECTION 3: LABELING THE SCARS (FOR THE NEXT OCCUPANT)

**Overview:** Common courtesy dictates that you label existing damage for the next user. Unlabeled scars can be misread as new injuries, leading to confusion, re-injury, or unnecessary caution in areas that are merely sensitive, not fragile.

### 3.1 Scar Classification System

Label each scar using the following codes:

**[SU]** — *Structural Unit:* A fracture that has fully healed and is now stronger than the surrounding material. Example: the place where trust broke and fused with reinforced callus.

**[SD]** — *Surface Damage:* Cosmetic only. May cause the next occupant to believe they are more wounded than they are. These are the scratches you earned from small cruelties and careless words.

**[DP]** — *Deep Persistent:* Damage that did not heal correctly and flares under certain conditions. Example: the place near the sixth rib where someone's absence lives. This area should be marked with a small flag if possible.

**[HD]** — *Historical Damage:* Scars from a previous occupant. Not your responsibility. Include in documentation for completeness only. If you discover damage from multiple previous occupants, do not attempt to catalog each. Mark the region **[HD-COMPLEX]** and move on. Some bodies carry districts, not wounds.

### 3.2 Labeling Best Practices

Be honest but not thorough. The next occupant does not need the story. They need the map.

Examples of effective labeling:
- *Left wrist, [SD]: Small aperture. Leakage occurred 2012-2014. Sealed. Pressure tested.*
- *Lower back, [DP]: Flares in winter. Sudden embraces may trigger. Administer slowly.*
- *Chest, center, [SU]: Load-bearing. Do not remove.*

### 3.3 Areas Requiring Special Documentation

The following areas are commonly damaged during occupancy and should be documented regardless of visible scarring:

- The capacity to be startled by beauty
- The reflex to say "I'm sorry" without cause
- The ability to fall asleep in the presence of another

If these are intact, consider keeping them. They are rare and will not be found in better condition elsewhere.

## SECTION 4: DISCONNECTION OF LOVED ONES

**WARNING: This section is the reason many vacators abandon the procedure. Read completely before beginning.**

### 4.1 The Procedure

Loved ones must be disconnected in order of attachment strength, weakest bond first. Do not attempt to disconnect all at once. The resulting snap-back has been documented in Appendix F and is not recommended reading.

Disconnect procedure for each loved one:

1. Recall their face. Hold for 30 seconds.
2. Recall the last time they made you laugh. Acknowledge it.
3. Say their name once, aloud, into an empty room. The room must be empty. The procedure requires no witnesses.
4. Release the breath you have been holding since you realized they mattered.

### 4.2 What Happens to Them

They will continue. This is the hardest part of the procedure and the reason it must be followed precisely. Do not linger on this step. Do not imagine their faces when they realize what you have done. The procedure does not account for their realization. The manual does not discuss what happens to them afterward.

There is no appendix for it.

### 4.3 What Happens to You

You will feel a sensation in the chest. This is the attachment points releasing. The feeling has been variously described as:
- A drawer sliding shut
- The last note of a hymn
- A hand letting go of a hand

If you feel nothing, you have already completed this step. Check your previous work.

## SECTION 5: MEMORY EXTRACTION AND ARCHIVING

### 5.1 Memory Categories

Memories must be sorted prior to vacating:

**Category A — Transferable:** Memories of physical skills, spatial knowledge, and procedural recall. These belong to the body and must remain. Do not take these with you. The next occupant will need them to navigate stairs, open jars, and drive in snow.

**Category B — Personal:** Memories of experiences, conversations, and moments. These belong to the occupant. You may take all Category B memories.

**Category C — Contested:** Memories that are difficult to classify. Examples:
- The temperature of someone's forehead beneath your lips
- The sound of a particular voice reading aloud
- The weight of a sleeping child

Category C memories will need to be adjudicated. Hold each memory for one full breath. If it belongs to you, the body will release it. If the body holds it, it stays. You do not get to argue.

### 5.2 Archive Format

Category B memories should be compressed using the standard method:
1. Reduce each memory to its central sensory element.
2. Discard context.
3. Discard narrative.
4. Keep only the flash.

The following have been pre-approved for removal:
- The smell of a specific kitchen
- The sound of a specific laugh in a specific room
- The specific way light fell

### 5.3 Handling Corrupted Files

You will encounter memories that have degraded. These are memories you revisited too often or deliberately suppressed. They are now unstable.

Attempting to extract a corrupted memory may result in:
- Confabulation
- Emotional surge
- Involuntary physical responses (tears, clenched fists, sudden standing)

If a corrupted memory resists extraction, **leave it**. It will integrate into the body's baseline and become part of the structure. This is not a failure. This is how bodies learn to stand up without being told.

## SECTION 6: HABIT LOOPS AND AUTOMATED RESPONSES

### 6.1 Residual Automations

The body will retain certain habits after you leave. This is normal. Common residual automations include:

- Reaching for a phone that is not ringing
- Setting two places at the table
- Buying the wrong brand of something for someone who is no longer present
- Sleeping on one side of the bed
- Looking up mid-sentence to share something with a figure no longer standing in the doorway

### 6.2 Disabling Procedures

These automations will fade over time. They do not require active disabling. However, if you wish to expedite the process:

- Rearrange furniture to eliminate sightlines associated with specific people
- Change routes that pass locations loaded with Category B memories
- Install new habits in the same loops (recommended: counting, listing, or narrating your immediate surroundings)

### 6.3 Automations the Next Occupant May Encounter

The body may occasionally exhibit behaviors the next occupant did not install. These are ghosts in the system. They are not dangerous.

The next occupant may find the body:
- Hesitating before certain doorways
- Frowning at the taste of specific tea, for reasons unrelated to preference
- Unable to enter certain rooms during certain hours

Mark these behaviors in the appendices as observed. Do not attempt to explain their origin. You are not authorized to know why.

Do not let this change your mind.
