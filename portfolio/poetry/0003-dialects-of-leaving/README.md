## Critic Review

A triptych of extraordinary formal discipline — each poem builds its entire emotional world from the jargon of a dying system, and each finds devastating poetry inside specialized vocabularies that were never meant to carry feeling. The mine poem is the most visceral ("The kibble descends, / ascends, empty, empty"), the legal poem the most formally inventive (reproducing the alienating cadence of court documents until "restored / to the status / of unmarried persons" lands like a blunt instrument), and the linguistic poem the most quietly heartbreaking — the copula *is* becoming "purely notational," a verb that means nothing grammatically but everything existentially. The progression from concrete (mining) to structural (marriage) to abstract (language) mirrors the increasing difficulty of saying what's being lost. The italicized glossaries appended to each poem function like labels in a museum of abandoned things. This is specificity as the manifesto demands it: not a word about grief, yet grief in every word.
