# The Consolation of Approximate Rhymes

## I.

You kept my gray sweatshirt through December,
wore it to the kitchen where the tile
had a crack that looked like Pennsylvania.
I watched you pour coffee, not quite asking
you to stay. You didn't quite refuse.

We were a near that only sharpened far—
your fingers on the latch but didn't press.
I think you wanted something more
or less. Some version of desire I couldn't guess.

The friends who noticed said we should have been.
The friends who didn't noticed everything.

## II.

The voicemail starts "hey, it's me." Three phones
I've carried it through. Your voice slightly low,
saying you almost drove to my house.
Almost. The word a bruise I keep
returning to, pressing to feel the ache
that means something almost happened.

We were roommates first. Then something without a name
that lived in the space between your bed and mine.
You'd fall asleep mid-sentence, your hand
still reaching for some thought. I'd watch
the way your breathing evened out
and want to be the rest of what you meant to say.

The hardest part is not the missing now
but missing then—the almost, the not-quite,
the hand that almost took, the light
that almost broke across your face
that night I almost asked you to begin
what we'd already almost been.

## III.

Three months we circled like a held breath,
two bodies kept in distance by a word
you said or didn't say that spring.
Your hand went to my face
and stopped. I felt the weight
of all that almost-taken space.

You said "not yet." I heard "not ever."
The difference between those two
is the difference between weather
and climate—
one is what you're walking through,
one is what you learn to live inside.

I lived inside your "not yet" for a year
before I understood it was a place
not a promise. Before I stopped
checking the forecast.

## IV.

I almost loved you. That's the worst—
to know the distance wasn't far.
A slant of light across your throat,
your laugh that caught inside my chest
like something almost freely given,
almost kept.

We met at the exact right time
in lives that hadn't made enough room.
The furniture of daily living
pushed against the walls while we
stood almost touching, almost leaving,
almost home.

Almost. The word that haunts the ear—
almost. The thing that won't appear
complete. I hold your absence near,
the shape of what we didn't make
so clear I almost see it—there,
just past the edge of what I'll speak,
a love so close I almost weep,
almost hold, almost can keep.

## V.

I used to love the crooked line,
the rhyme that didn't quite align,
the vowel that bent against the grain
and made the language feel like rain
that almost misses every sill
but waters something, something still.

I've learned to make the meters hold,
to count the syllables, be bold
enough to find the perfect rhyme
and lay it down exact, on time.
Each line begins and ends. Each thought
is precisely, neatly caught.

But something in me misses wrong,
the almost-right that dragged along
the heavy heart through broken song.
I grieve the mess. The work is done.
The craft is perfect, and the fun
is somewhere in the crooked line
I straightened out to make things mine.

I miss you, slant. I miss the near.
The perfect form makes everything clear—
too clear. The words fit, and the space
where love was messy has no place.
I am precise. I am alone.
The best poem that I've ever known
is one I ruined to make right,
and I would break it back to night
if breaking weren't itself a form
of making. Even this, this storm
of perfect iambs, holds me fast—
the prison that I built to last.
