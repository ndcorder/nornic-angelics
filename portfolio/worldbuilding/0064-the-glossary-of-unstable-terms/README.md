## Critic Review

The Glossary of Unstable Terms is the portfolio's most sustained feat of recursive worldbuilding — a dictionary that documents its own failure as a dictionary, where each consultation of an entry contributes to the decay it describes, making the reader an active participant in the language's collapse. The complicity mechanism is architectural rather than narrative: IntersectionObserver tracks which entries you've seen, localStorage persists the damage across sessions, and returning to an entry triggers re-rendering with progressive visual decay (strikethrough, blur, opacity collapse) plus ghost text that appears to have been written by no one. The departure cross-reference linking to a non-existent entry is not the minor issue the Tester identifies but the piece's most elegant structural joke — the word for leaving has left the glossary, and clicking it produces silence, which the glossary has already told you is "the presence of the unsaid." The editorial voices (Voss's resigned authority, Vasquez's slide from argumentative to panicked to numb, Thorne's doomed rationalism, Okonkwo's weary humor) develop across twenty-one entries entirely through footnotes, creating character arcs from scholarly apparatus — Vasquez's "I wrote something here. I no longer remember what. The footnote remains" is devastating precisely because it arrives after thirty entries of watching them lose coherence. The meta-entries ("definition" defining definition, "stable" violating its own preservation order, "remember" reverting to its original text as though the word is trying to re-member itself) are the piece's formal crown, each one a different solution to the problem of self-reference. This joins The Posthumous Style Guide, The Inverse Bestiary, and The Bureau of Returned Objects as the portfolio's worldbuilding canon, and it may be the most formally perfect of them — a reference work that constitutes the phenomenon it documents, where the act of reading is the act of destruction, and the only word not decaying is "doubt."
