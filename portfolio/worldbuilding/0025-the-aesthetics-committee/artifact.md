## PART I: EVALUATION RUBRIC
### Public Visual Expression Assessment Instrument (Revised Season 43)

**SCORING:** Each criterion rated 1–5. Minimum passing composite: 18/35.

---

**CRITERION A: Cohesion with Surrounding Visual Field (1–5)**

Does the proposed expression complement or disrupt the established architectural vernacular? Assessors should consider color palette continuity, material harmony, scale proportionality, and whether the expression acknowledges the visual rhythm of adjacent properties. Reference Municipal Design Code §7.3.

*5 = Enhances existing visual harmony*
*3 = Neutral impact on visual field*
*1 = Significant visual disruption*

**CRITERION B: Temporal Boundedness (1–5)**

Does the proposed expression have a defined end point? Expressions without clear temporal boundaries tend toward visual stagnation and emotional hoarding. Assessors should note whether the petitioner has specified a removal date or seasonal transition plan.

*5 = Clear endpoint articulated*
*3 = Implicit or natural endpoint*
*1 = No endpoint; indefinite persistence anticipated*

**CRITERION C: Public Accessibility of Meaning (1–5)**

Can the expression be reasonably interpreted by a viewer with no prior knowledge of the petitioner's circumstances? Private languages, insider references, and highly individualized symbol systems score lower. The committee maintains that public space requires a baseline of shared legibility.

*5 = Immediately legible to any passerby*
*3 = Interpretable with moderate effort*
*1 = Opaque without petitioner-specific context*

**CRITERION D: Maintenance Commitment (1–5)**

Has the petitioner demonstrated a plan for upkeep? Expressions that degrade through neglect become visual liabilities. This includes weathering, fading, structural compromise, and organic decay.

*5 = Detailed maintenance plan with materials list*
*3 = General commitment to upkeep*
*1 = No maintenance plan articulated*

**CRITERION E: Emotional Calibration (1–5)**

Does the proposed expression occupy the designated emotional range for public display? Assessors should evaluate whether the expression's emotional intensity is appropriate for its proposed location and duration. Reference the Emotional Spectrum Guidelines (Appendix C). Overwhelming positive affect is regulated identically to overwhelming negative affect.

*5 = Calibrated to moderate emotional range*
*3 = Occasional intensity spikes within acceptable parameters*
*1 = Sustained emotional intensity exceeding public threshold*

**CRITERION F: Distinction from Memorial Infrastructure (1–5)**

Can the expression be distinguished from municipally sanctioned memorial infrastructure (benches, plaques, commemorative plantings)? Confusion between private expression and civic memorial dilutes both systems.

*5 = Clearly distinct from civic infrastructure*
*3 = Some overlap but distinguishable on inspection*
*1 = Indistinguishable from municipal memorial*

**CRITERION G: Precedent Consistency (1–5)**

Does granting this application create inconsistencies with prior rulings? Assessors should consult the precedent database (Season 38–present) for comparable cases.

*5 = Consistent with established precedent*
*3 = Novel but not contradictory*
*1 = Directly contradicts prior ruling*

---

**ADDITIONAL NOTES:**

* Applications scoring below 10/35 may be denied without full committee review at the Chair's discretion.
* Applications scoring between 18–22/35 require a conditions attachment.
* Dissenting opinions must be filed within 72 hours of ruling publication.
* Petitioners may reapply after one full season following denial.

### CASE FILE 44-F-112
**Petitioner:** Luz Dominguez, 8 Verrada Way
**Application Type:** Garden Preservation (Winter Extension)
**Date Filed:** Season 44, Late Autumn
**Status:** DENIED

**COMMITTEE ROSTER:**
Chair: Mariana Voss
Assessor: David Kwon
Assessor: Hadia El-Amin
Assessor: Thomas Reiter
Assessor: Sonya Park

**PETITIONER'S WRITTEN STATEMENT:**

*In response to Form Item 4 ("Describe the proposed expression in spatial and material terms"):*

It is a garden. It has always been a garden. The tomatoes are done now but the marigolds still have color. The rosemary will survive if I cover it. He built the planters himself from the limestone that came out of the ground when they dug the foundation for the Verameer overpass. That limestone is from here. He is from here. The garden is from here. I want to leave it standing through the cold months and add evergreen boughs and dried hydrangea and the ceramic pots he painted.

*In response to Form Item 7 ("Explain how the proposed expression serves the public visual interest"):*

I think it serves the interest of being a person who lives somewhere and makes that somewhere more alive. I think people walking past should see that someone cared about a small patch of ground for twelve years. I think that is not nothing. I think seeing a garden in winter is a way of seeing that someone believed in spring many times and was right.

*In response to Form Item 9 ("Describe any anticipated emotional impact on the surrounding community"):*

My husband Mateo is dead. The garden is the only thing he left unfinished and I am asking you to let me finish it through winter.

**ASSESSMENT:**

**Criterion A (Cohesion):** 4/5 — The existing garden structure is consistent with the visual vernacular. Planters use locally sourced stone. Color palette has historically been appropriate. Deductions for the proposed addition of ceramic pots of inconsistent glaze.

**Criterion B (Temporal Boundedness):** 2/5 — Petitioner requests "through the cold months" but does not specify a removal date. Garden infrastructure (planters, pots) would likely persist into spring, creating an indefinite visual condition.

**Criterion C (Accessibility of Meaning):** 2/5 — The garden, viewed without petitioner's statement, reads as an ordinary garden in a state of seasonal neglect. The emotional meaning is entirely dependent on petitioner-specific context. A passerby would not interpret a winter garden as an expression of grief.

**Criterion D (Maintenance):** 3/5 — Petitioner has historically maintained the garden to an acceptable standard. However, winter maintenance plan is vague ("cover the rosemary").

**Criterion E (Emotional Calibration):** 2/5 — Assessment of petitioner's written statement suggests sustained emotional intensity exceeding the public threshold. The committee acknowledges the gravity of the petitioner's loss but notes that the application, if granted, would effectively convert private grief into a permanent visual fixture.

**Criterion F (Memorial Distinction):** 1/5 — The proposed expression is indistinguishable from a commemorative planting. The committee notes that Mateo Dominguez does not qualify for municipal memorial status under Municipal Code §12.1 (required minimum ten years of documented civic contribution).

**Criterion G (Precedent):** 3/5 — Novel application. No directly comparable prior ruling.

**COMPOSITE SCORE:** 17/35 (below minimum threshold of 18)

**RULING:** DENIED

**CONDITIONS IF REAPPLYING:** Petitioner may submit a revised application for a time-limited seasonal display (maximum 90 days) using municipally approved winter planting materials, with a written removal commitment. Petitioner must remove all commemorative elements (ceramic pots, painted stones) from public-facing portions of the property.

### CASE FILE 45-E-037
**Petitioner:** J. (minor), 14 Sagrada Loop
**Application Type:** Temporary Mural (Exterior Wall, North-Facing)
**Date Filed:** Season 45, Early Spring
**Status:** GRANTED WITH CONDITIONS

**COMMITTEE ROSTER:**
Chair: Mariana Voss
Assessor: David Kwon
Assessor: Hadia El-Amin
Assessor: Thomas Reiter
Assessor: Sonya Park

**PETITIONER'S WRITTEN STATEMENT:**

*In response to Form Item 4 ("Describe the proposed expression in spatial and material terms"):*

My dog Rafa has been missing since Season 44 Late Winter. The north wall of our house faces the street. I want to paint his picture there so maybe someone will see it and recognize him. I'm not a very good painter but I know what he looks like. He has one ear that goes up and one that stays down. He is brown with a white chest. I would paint him with his tongue out because that is how he always was.

*In response to Form Item 7 ("Explain how the proposed expression serves the public visual interest"):*

Maybe someone will see him and know where he is. Maybe they will say "I saw that dog on that wall and I saw him in real life." Also it would make me feel like he is still going to come home. The wall is just grey right now.

*In response to Form Item 9 ("Describe any anticipated emotional impact on the surrounding community"):*

People like dogs. People feel sad about missing dogs. Maybe they will feel sad and then they will look for him and then they will feel happy. My mom says hope is not the same as lying and I think that is right.

**ASSESSMENT:**

**Criterion A (Cohesion):** 3/5 — Proposed mural is on a currently blank grey wall. Neutral impact. Petitioner's self-assessed skill level suggests the mural will be roughly executed, but this is consistent with the residential vernacular of Sagrada Loop, which includes multiple hand-painted house numbers and informal signage.

**Criterion B (Temporal Boundedness):** 3/5 — Implicit endpoint: mural becomes unnecessary if dog is recovered. Petitioner has not committed to removal in the event the dog is not recovered.

**Criterion C (Accessibility of Meaning):** 5/5 — A painting of a missing dog is immediately legible to any viewer.

**Criterion D (Maintenance):** 2/5 — No maintenance plan. Petitioner is a minor and has not addressed weatherproofing or material durability. Wall faces north; mural will weather rapidly.

**Criterion E (Emotional Calibration):** 4/5 — Moderate emotional range. Hope and sadness are within the acceptable spectrum.

**Criterion F (Memorial Distinction):** 4/5 — Clearly distinct from municipal memorial infrastructure. No reasonable viewer would confuse a missing dog mural with a commemorative plaque or bench.

**Criterion G (Precedent):** 3/5 — Novel application, but consistent with Season 42 informal signage ruling (Case 42-C-089, "Lost Cat" flyers).

**COMPOSITE SCORE:** 24/35

**RULING:** GRANTED WITH CONDITIONS

**CONDITIONS OF APPROVAL:**

1. Mural must not exceed 1.2 meters in any dimension.
2. Petitioner must use municipally approved exterior paint (color palette 7B-14 available at procurement office). Petitioner's proposed brown is not available in palette 7B-14; closest approved equivalent is Umber Standard (sample swatch attached).
3. Mural must be removed no later than Season 46 Early Summer (approximately 15 months from date of approval), regardless of outcome.
4. Text overlay is limited to the word "MISSING" and a contact number. No additional text, including the animal's name.
5. Mural must be painted within the designated marking zone on the wall (see attached diagram, Zone C).
6. Petitioner must submit a removal plan signed by a parent or guardian.
7. If the mural degrades by more than 40% (assessed visually by committee representative) before the removal date, it must be repainted to standard or removed immediately.

**NOTE:** The committee acknowledges that the approved color palette may not accurately represent the petitioner's dog.

### CASE FILE 44-K-201
**Petitioner:** Erik Karsh, 31 Merchant Street
**Application Type:** Behavioral Expression (Lawn)
**Date Filed:** Season 44, Mid-Summer
**Status:** DENIED

**COMMITTEE ROSTER:**
Chair: Mariana Voss
Assessor: David Kwon
Assessor: Hadia El-Amin
Assessor: Thomas Reiter
Assessor: Sonya Park

**PETITIONER'S WRITTEN STATEMENT:**

*In response to Form Item 4 ("Describe the proposed expression in spatial and material terms"):*

I want to sit on my lawn and cry. Not every day. Some days. I will sit on the grass and I will cry. I will not erect anything. I will not paint anything. I will not leave anything behind. I will sit and then I will go back inside.

*In response to Form Item 7 ("Explain how the proposed expression serves the public visual interest"):*

I don't think the public visual interest is served by pretending people don't cry on their lawns.

*In response to Form Item 9 ("Describe any anticipated emotional impact on the surrounding community"):*

Someone might see me and feel something. That is what happens when you see a person.

**ASSESSMENT:**

**Criterion A (Cohesion):** N/A — No physical alteration to visual field proposed.

**Criterion B (Temporal Boundedness):** 2/5 — Petitioner describes "some days" without schedule or endpoint.

**Criterion C (Accessibility of Meaning):** 2/5 — A person sitting on a lawn is not legible as a deliberate visual expression. A person crying on a lawn is legible as personal distress, not as a designed aesthetic statement. The committee does not regulate personal distress per se, but a deliberate public display of personal distress falls within the committee's jurisdiction when the petitioner has filed formal notice of intent to perform that display.

**Criterion D (Maintenance):** N/A — No physical installation proposed.

**Criterion E (Emotional Calibration):** 1/5 — Petitioner explicitly proposes to display sustained emotional distress (crying) in a public-facing space. This exceeds the public threshold regardless of the committee's sympathy for the petitioner's circumstances.

**Criterion F (Memorial Distinction):** N/A

**Criterion G (Precedent):** 2/5 — Season 41 case 41-B-156 (public prayer on residential stoop) established that intentional, scheduled, or repeated behavioral displays in public-facing spaces fall within the committee's jurisdiction. This application extends that logic to emotional expression without a visual artifact, which the committee finds troubling but jurisdictionally sound under current municipal code.

**COMPOSITE SCORE:** 7/35 (below Chair's summary denial threshold of 10)

**RULING:** DENIED (Chair's summary authority)

**NOTE:** The committee wishes to clarify that this ruling does not prohibit the petitioner from experiencing or expressing emotion on his own property. It prohibits the *formalization* of that expression through the municipal application process, which constitutes a deliberate public visual act. The petitioner is free to sit on his lawn at any time without committee approval, provided he does not represent his actions as a sanctioned public expression. The committee regulates the formal proposal, not the informal act.

### CASE FILE 45-H-088
**Petitioner:** Sonia Hadid, 22 Caldera Street
**Application Type:** Window Display (Interior, Street-Visible)
**Date Filed:** Season 45, Late Spring
**Status:** GRANTED

**COMMITTEE ROSTER:**
Chair: Mariana Voss
Assessor: David Kwon (Dissenting)
Assessor: Hadia El-Amin
Assessor: Thomas Reiter
Assessor: Sonya Park

**PETITIONER'S WRITTEN STATEMENT:**

*In response to Form Item 4:*

I want to put things in my front window. My daughter Lena was born in this house and she will be born again in this house. She is arriving in Early Autumn. I want to arrange things that will make her first sight of the world a beautiful one: a yellow dress my mother made, a glass bird I found at the market on Reconciliation Day, some dried flowers, a string of lights, and a drawing my husband made of her before he met her. It is a drawing of a face with no features because he does not know what she looks like yet.

*In response to Form Item 7:*

The things in the window will be beautiful. That is all I know how to say. They will be beautiful and she will see them.

*In response to Form Item 9:*

I hope it will make people feel the way I feel, which is that something very good is about to happen and it has been a long time coming.

**ASSESSMENT:**

**Criterion A (Cohesion):** 5/5 — Window displays are consistent with the visual vernacular of Caldera Street. Proposed materials are soft, domestic, and appropriately scaled. The yellow dress will complement the limestone facade at 22 Caldera.

**Criterion B (Temporal Boundedness):** 5/5 — Petitioner specifies the display will be removed when the child is born and brought home. Clear, natural endpoint.

**Criterion C (Accessibility of Meaning):** 4/5 — A collection of domestic objects in a window is legible as celebration or anticipation. The drawing may be somewhat opaque, but the overall composition communicates positive expectation.

**Criterion D (Maintenance):** 4/5 — Indoor display; minimal weather exposure. Petitioner has stated she will refresh the dried flowers if they fade.

**Criterion E (Emotional Calibration):** 4/5 — Joy and anticipation are within the approved emotional spectrum.

**Criterion F (Memorial Distinction):** 5/5 — Celebration display. No confusion with memorial infrastructure.

**Criterion G (Precedent):** 4/5 — Consistent with Season 43 window display approval (Case 43-D-072, seasonal decorative arrangement).

**COMPOSITE SCORE:** 31/35

**RULING:** GRANTED

---

**DISSENTING OPINION — Assessor David Kwon:**

I vote to approve this application. I am filing this dissent not to contest the ruling but to contest the committee's existence.

I have served on this committee for six seasons. In that time I have reviewed 247 applications. I have approved 198 and denied 49. I have read descriptions of grief, hope, love, and desperation translated into the language of spatial and material terms because we require that translation. I have scored a woman's garden on a scale of one to five. I have told a man that he may cry on his lawn informally but not formally. I have told a child that her dog is the wrong brown.

I am not dissenting from this ruling. Sonia Hadid should put things in her window. I am dissenting from the system that requires her to ask permission.

The committee was established to maintain visual coherence in the aftermath of the Reconciliation, when the city needed to reestablish a shared sense of order. That was eleven seasons ago. We are no longer in crisis. What we have built is not order but a mechanism for the conversion of private feeling into public compliance.

I am aware that this dissent will be filed and ignored, as my previous dissents have been filed and ignored. I am aware that I could resign and that my resignation would be more coherent than a dissent arguing for the committee's dissolution. But I remain because I believe the person who reads these applications should be someone who finds them unbearable. My predecessors found them manageable. I do not. That is the most useful thing I can offer.

I recommend that the committee be dissolved and that the visual regulation of private expression be limited to existing municipal codes governing structural safety and public obstruction. I recommend that we stop asking people to justify their feelings in spatial and material terms. I recommend that we let the woman keep her garden.

—David Kwon, Assessor, Season 39–present

**MEMORANDUM**
**TO:** Aesthetics Committee, All Members
**FROM:** Mariana Voss, Chair
**DATE:** Season 44, Mid-Summer
**RE:** Application Volume and Emotional Calibration

Colleagues,

I want to address something informally before it becomes a formal concern.

Our application volume has increased 34% this season. I have reviewed the pending applications and I want to note a pattern that the rubric does not fully account for: several petitioners appear to be testing the boundaries of the emotional calibration criterion by proposing expressions that sit just at the threshold of acceptable intensity.

I am not suggesting we change the rubric. The rubric was revised in Season 43 after extensive public comment and it serves us well. But I want to remind the committee that our role is not to evaluate the merit of a petitioner's feelings. Our role is to evaluate the suitability of a particular visual expression for public space. These are different tasks. I am concerned that we are sometimes conflating them.

When Erik Karsh applied for permission to cry on his lawn, I denied the application under my summary authority because the application itself — not the act, but the *formal request* — constituted a deliberate public visual act that exceeded the emotional calibration threshold. But I want to acknowledge that the denial read poorly. It read as if we were telling a man he could not cry. We were not. We were telling him that the act of asking permission transformed a private moment into a public statement, and that public statement did not meet our criteria.

I recognize the distinction is thin. I recognize it may be too thin.

I am not suggesting we change the rubric. I am suggesting that we be precise in our language and compassionate in our notes, and that we remember that the petitioners read everything we write.

—Mariana

**MEMORANDUM**
**TO:** Mariana Voss, Chair
**FROM:** David Kwon, Assessor
**DATE:** Season 45, Late Autumn
**RE:** Case 45-H-088 and the question of proportionality

Mariana,

I am filing this as a memo rather than a dissent because I am not dissenting from the ruling. I want to address something that has been bothering me since the Karsh denial and that the Hadid approval has made more acute.

Sonia Hadid scored 31/35. Her application was excellent by every measure the rubric provides. She will put beautiful things in her window and her daughter will see them. This is good.

Luz Dominguez scored 17/35. She wanted to keep a garden through the winter. One point below the threshold. We denied her application and told her to remove the ceramic pots her husband made.

I want to be clear about what we are doing. We are not regulating visual coherence. We are drawing a line between kinds of love and deciding which ones are allowed to be visible. Hadid's love is anticipatory and joyful and she scored 31. Dominguez's love is grieving and she scored 17. Karsh's love is bewildered and he scored 7.

The rubric does not mention love. It mentions cohesion, temporal boundedness, accessibility, maintenance, calibration, memorial distinction, and precedent. But every application we receive is a description of love in the language of spatial and material terms, because that is what we ask for. And our rubric, which does not mention love, reliably scores joyful love higher than grieving love.

I do not think this is intentional. I think it is structural. The rubric values legibility, and joy is more legible than grief. The rubric values calibration, and grief does not calibrate well. The rubric values temporal boundedness, and grief does not end on schedule.

I am not asking you to change the rubric. I am asking you to see what it does.

—David

## PART IV: APPENDIX

### Architectural and Municipal Terms

**The Reconciliation:** A civic crisis approximately eleven seasons ago (exact details unspecified in available committee records) during which, per Chair Voss's testimony, "every surface became a canvas for unprocessed feeling." The committee was established in the aftermath.

**Seasons:** The municipality organizes time into seasons. Each season has three periods: Early, Mid, and Late. Four seasons per year. Current year unspecified in available records.

**Caldera Street:** Residential street characterized by limestone facades and domestic-scale architecture. Sonia Hadid's residence is located here.

**Sagrada Loop:** Residential street with a more informal visual vernacular, including hand-painted house numbers and informal signage.

**Verrada Way:** Location of the Dominguez residence and Mateo Dominguez's garden.

**Merchant Street:** Location of Erik Karsh's residence.

**Avenida Mercado:** Location of Tamara Voss's residence. Note: the street name suggests a market district, but the residence is described as having a porch facing the street.

**The Verameer Overpass:** Infrastructure project that generated the limestone used by Mateo Dominguez to build his garden planters.

**Municipal Design Code §7.3:** "The street is a composition, not a canvas." Frequently cited in committee deliberations.

**Municipal Code §12.1:** Requires minimum ten years of documented civic contribution to qualify for municipal memorial status.

**Emotional Spectrum Guidelines (Appendix C):** Referenced in the rubric but not included in this archive. Defines the acceptable range of emotional intensity for public visual expression.

**Palette 7B-14:** Municipally approved exterior paint color palette. Does not include the color brown most accurate to the petitioner's missing dog.
