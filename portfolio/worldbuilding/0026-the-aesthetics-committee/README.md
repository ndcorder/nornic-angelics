## Critic Review

The Aesthetics Committee is the portfolio's worldbuilding peak and one of its most devastating artifacts — a municipal bureaucracy that regulates the visual expression of private feeling, rendered in such perfect institutional register that every act of compassion reads as cruelty and every act of cruelty reads as procedure. The specificity is extraordinary and never decorative: the staples not to exceed four in number, the "beseeching" language prohibited on missing dog posters, the Committee-approved slate violet that replaces a widow's deep plum, the fifteen-business-day processing time for an Emotional Display Permit "with documentation." The rubric itself is a masterwork of structural bias — six criteria that structurally disadvantage grief (visible, unbounded, poorly calibrated to neighborhood tone) while rewarding cheerful expressions (contained, moderate, seasonally appropriate) — and Vasquez's dissenting note that "no petition has ever been denied for being insufficiently sad" articulates the asymmetry the Committee cannot see. The escalation from minor-comic conditions on a crayon portrait through warmly-approved child's mural and absurdist wind chime restrictions to the garden denied for rot and the fence permitted only Committee-approved grief shades to Helen Brandt's petition to cry on her own lawn — denied, referred to the Office of Community Wellness — is architectural, each ruling a step further into the system's logic until you reach the memorandum where a dissenting member writes "that is what lawns are for, Margaux. That is what the world is for" and you realize the entire document has been building to that sentence. The final line — "The Committee governs what is built, placed, painted, planted, or installed — not what is felt" — is the cruelest stroke: a reassurance that is an admission, a boundary that is a confession. This pairs with Termination for Convenience and Soft Launch as the portfolio's triptych on bureaucratic erasure, but where those forged documents and servers, this one forges an entire civic apparatus. The worldbuilding domain gap is filled at the highest possible level.
