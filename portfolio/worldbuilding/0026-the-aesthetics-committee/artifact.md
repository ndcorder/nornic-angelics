## II. INTERNAL RUBRIC — "STANDARDS FOR THE EVALUATION OF PUBLIC AESTHETIC EXPRESSION"

*(Municipal Code §7.12, Appendix B — last amended September 2019)*

All petitions are evaluated against the following criteria. Each criterion is scored on a scale of 1 (Deficient) to 5 (Exemplary). A minimum composite score of **18/30** is required for approval. Scores of 14–17 qualify for **conditional approval** subject to Committee-specified modifications. Scores below 14 result in **denial**.

**A. COHESION (1–5)**
Does the expression maintain visual and tonal harmony with its immediate surroundings (within a 50-foot radius)? Consider materials, color palette, scale, and emotional register.

**B. TEMPORAL APPROPRIATENESS (1–5)**
Is the expression suited to the current season, acknowledged civic mood, and/or relevant calendar period? Expressions invoking loss may receive seasonal adjustments per §7.34(b).

**C. MATERIAL DURABILITY (1–5)**
Will the proposed materials maintain aesthetic integrity under normal weather conditions for the requested duration? Unprotected paper goods, organic matter subject to rapid decomposition, and materials that shed, bleed, or stain score automatically at 1 unless mitigations are proposed.

**D. EMOTIONAL TONALITY (1–5)**
Does the expression's emotional register fall within the neighborhood's designated tonal range? Reference the most recent Neighborhood Tonal Assessment (filed annually). Expressions that introduce dissonant affect without architectural buffering will score below 3.

**E. PUBLIC SAFETY AND OBSTRUCTION (1–5)**
Does the expression impede pedestrian flow, vehicular sightlines, utility access, or general right-of-way? Any obstruction of a public thoroughfare exceeding 24 inches in any dimension scores at 2 or below.

**F. PRECEDENT COMPATIBILITY (1–5)**
Is the expression consistent with established Committee precedent? Novel expressions without precedent will be evaluated for their potential to establish *detrimental precedent* (see §7.56 — "The Floodgates Provision").

*Note: The Committee retains discretionary override authority per §7.11(a) to approve or deny any petition irrespective of composite score, subject to written justification entered into the record.*

### RULING 41-A
**Petitioner:** Marta Koslowski, 486 Arbor Lane
**Date Filed:** September 3
**Expression Requested:** Child's crayon portrait of a missing dog (male boxer, "Champion"), to be stapled to the telephone pole at the corner of Arbor Lane and 9th Street.

**Committee Score:**
| Criterion | Score |
|---|---|
| Cohesion | 3 |
| Temporal Appropriateness | 4 |
| Material Durability | 1 |
| Emotional Tonality | 4 |
| Public Safety and Obstruction | 5 |
| Precedent Compatibility | 3 |
| **Total** | **20** |

**Ruling:** APPROVED WITH CONDITIONS

1. The portrait must be laminated or otherwise weather-sealed within seven (7) days of posting. Unprotected paper in any form is not permitted beyond a 72-hour grace period.
2. The portrait must be removed after ninety (90) calendar days from the date of posting, or upon the dog's return, whichever comes first. In the event of the dog's confirmed death, the portrait may remain for an additional fourteen (14) days as a mourning period, after which it must be removed.
3. Staples are not to exceed four (4) in number and must be placed no higher than 60 inches from ground level to permit removal by the petitioner without a ladder.
4. The portrait may include the word "MISSING" and a contact number but must not include exclamation points, the word "PLEASE," or any language that could be classified as *beseeching* per the Committee's guidelines on public desperation (§7.44(c)).

**Chair Ferdinandsen's Note:** "The portrait itself is charming and, in its naiveté, cohesive with the residential character of Arbor Lane. The Committee's conditions are logistical, not aesthetic. We wish Champion a safe return."

### RULING 42-B
**Petitioner:** Grace Oduya, 203 Vine Street
**Date Filed:** October 7
**Expression Requested:** Permission to leave her late husband Daniel Oduya's vegetable garden unharvested through the coming winter, as a visible acknowledgment of his passing (death date: August 14). The garden contains tomatoes, bell peppers, Swiss chard, and a row of sunflowers now past bloom.

**Committee Score:**
| Criterion | Score |
|---|---|
| Cohesion | 2 |
| Temporal Appropriateness | 2 |
| Material Durability | 1 |
| Emotional Tonality | 2 |
| Public Safety and Obstruction | 4 |
| Precedent Compatibility | 1 |
| **Total** | **12** |

**Ruling:** DENIED

**Summary Justification:** The Committee finds that the proposed expression — seasonal agricultural decay permitted to progress unimpeded through the winter months — exceeds the neighborhood rot threshold established in *Petition of Alvarado* (Ruling 29-F) and reinforced in *Petition of Nkemdirim* (Ruling 33-C). Unharvested vegetables, particularly soft-fruited crops such as tomatoes, undergo visible decomposition that constitutes an unpermitted blight aesthetic under §7.22(a). The Committee acknowledges the petitioner's grief and notes that a private memorial garden, enclosed by a fence of no less than 48 inches and not visible from the public right-of-way, would not require Committee review.

**Member Vasquez (dissenting):** "The garden is not rotting. It is grieving. Daniel Oduya planted those tomatoes in April. He tended them while he was dying. The sunflowers turned toward him while he sat on the porch. What Mrs. Oduya is asking for is one winter. One winter where the garden is allowed to do what gardens do when no one is left to tend them. The Neighborhood Rot Threshold — I will note that this phrase does not appear anywhere in the municipal code and was invented by this Committee in Ruling 29-F — is being applied here to a woman's loss. We are telling her that her husband's garden is too ugly to be seen dying. I dissent."

**Chair Ferdinandsen's Response to Dissent (entered into record):** "The dissent mischaracterizes the ruling. The Committee does not regulate grief. It regulates the public visibility of organic decomposition on residential properties visible from the street. Mrs. Oduya's loss is acknowledged. Her garden remains her own. What is visible from the public right-of-way is, by definition, not only her own."

### RULING 43-F
**Petitioner:** Janet Solis, 815 Meadowbrook Avenue
**Date Filed:** November 9
**Expression Requested:** Permission to paint her residential privacy fence (wood, approximately 62 linear feet, facing the public sidewalk on Meadowbrook Avenue) in what the petitioner describes as "a shade of grief." Petitioner submitted three color samples: a slate violet, a muted charcoal-blue, and a deep plum. The petitioner's husband, Marco Solis, died on July 2 of the current year. The fence is currently white.

**Committee Score:**
| Criterion | Score |
|---|---|
| Cohesion | 2 |
| Temporal Appropriateness | 3 |
| Material Durability | 5 |
| Emotional Tonality | 1 |
| Public Safety and Obstruction | 5 |
| Precedent Compatibility | 2 |
| **Total** | **18** |

**Ruling:** APPROVED WITH CONDITIONS

1. Of the three submitted samples, only the **slate violet (Sample A)** is approved. The muted charcoal-blue (Sample B) is denied as inconsistent with the prevailing fence tones on Meadowbrook Avenue (see §7.14 — Neighborhood Color Concordance). The deep plum (Sample C) is denied as exceeding the maximum permitted saturation for residential grief expressions per *Petition of Waverly* (Ruling 37-K).
2. The approved grief shade must be maintained in good condition. Fading, peeling, or weathering that results in a color shift beyond the Committee-approved sample will require repainting or removal within thirty (30) days of notification.
3. The petitioner may display the grief shade for a period not to exceed **eighteen (18) months** from the date of the spouse's death, after which the fence must be returned to white or repainted in a Committee-approved residential tone.
4. A second application may be filed if the petitioner wishes to extend the grief period, subject to a new hearing and a demonstrated emotional need letter from a licensed therapist or grief counselor.

**Member Vasquez (dissenting, partial):** "I approve the slate violet because Mrs. Solis should be allowed her color. But I object to the eighteen-month limitation. We do not tell people when to stop grieving. We should not tell them when to stop painting. I note that in the entire history of this Committee, no petition has ever been denied for being *insufficiently* sad. We regulate the expression of pain because pain is visible. Joy is never subjected to a time limit. I dissent from Condition 3."

### RULING 44-C
**Petitioner:** Helen Brandt, 5 Poplar Street
**Date Filed:** November 28
**Expression Requested:** Permission to sit on her front lawn and cry, "where people can see," during evening hours (approximately 6:00 PM to 8:00 PM), for an indefinite period following the death of her son, Pfc. Adam Brandt, killed in action on October 12. The petitioner's front lawn is approximately 15 feet from the public sidewalk. The petitioner has stated she does not intend to wail, moan, or make any vocal sounds beyond "what comes out."

**Committee Score:**
| Criterion | Score |
|---|---|
| Cohesion | 1 |
| Temporal Appropriateness | 1 |
| Material Durability | N/A |
| Emotional Tonality | 1 |
| Public Safety and Obstruction | 3 |
| Precedent Compatibility | 1 |
| **Total** | **7** |

**Ruling:** DENIED

**Summary Justification:** The Committee finds that the petitioner's request — to perform visible, sustained, unstructured grief upon a residential front lawn within public view — does not constitute a recognized aesthetic expression under Municipal Code §7.01. The Committee's authority extends to *artifacts, installations, modifications, and deliberate constructions* placed upon or affixed to property visible from the public right-of-way. A living person's unmediated emotional display, while acknowledged as deeply human, is not an *aesthetic expression* subject to the Committee's approval or denial. The Committee therefore lacks jurisdiction to grant this petition.

However, the Committee further finds that if such a display were to occur without approval, it would constitute an *unpermitted public affect event* under §7.71 (Emergency Emotional Display Provisions), and the Office of Community Wellness would be authorized to intervene.

The petitioner is advised to direct her request to the Office of Community Wellness, which may issue a temporary Emotional Display Permit under separate authority (§9.13 of the Municipal Health and Wellness Code). The Committee expresses its condolences.

**Member Vasquez (dissenting):** "I have served on this Committee for four years. I have approved wind chimes and denied vegetable gardens. I have placed conditions on crayon drawings. I have told a woman that her grief is the wrong shade of purple. I have done all of this because I believed — or tried to believe — that structure serves feeling. That form makes emotion legible. That we are not censors but editors. I no longer believe this. A mother wants to sit on her grass and cry where the world can see her, and we have told her she needs a *permit* from a *wellness office* to do it. We have said she needs *permission* to be seen breaking. I will not vote to deny this. I vote to approve. And I want the record to show that I was the only one. Helen Brandt's grief is not an aesthetic. It is not a product, an installation, a color sample, or a seasonal blight. It is a woman on her knees in the grass, and it should be allowed to be exactly what it is, for as long as it is, and every person who walks past should have to see it, because that is what loss looks like, and we have no right — no right at all — to pretend otherwise. I dissent. I dissent from this ruling, and I dissent from the rubric, and I dissent from the precedent that says a body in grief is not an expression but a violation."

**Chair Ferdinandsen's Response to Dissent (entered into record):** "The dissent is noted and entered in full. The Committee's ruling stands. The petitioner is advised to contact the Office of Community Wellness at the number provided in the attached appendix."

## IV. CITATED PRECEDENTS — INDEX

*The following precedents were referenced in the above rulings and are provided here in summary for public reference.*

**Ruling 29-F, *Petition of Alvarado*:**
Petitioner requested permission to allow a fruit tree (plum) to drop fruit uncollected on a public-adjacent lawn through autumn as a "natural decay installation." Denied. Established the *Neighborhood Rot Threshold* — a non-statutory standard adopted by the Committee defining the permissible degree of visible organic decomposition on residential properties.

**Ruling 33-C, *Petition of Nkemdirim*:**
Petitioner requested permission to erect a small shrine (candles, photographs, dried flowers) on a park bench following the death of a friend. Denied; public park fixtures may not be modified for private memorial use. Dissent filed (Member Braithwaite) on grounds that the shrine was temporary and modest. Dissent overruled.

**Ruling 37-K, *Petition of Waverly*:**
Petitioner requested permission to paint the exterior of her home in what she described as "the color of a bruise" — a deep, mottled purple-blue — following a domestic violence incident. Denied on saturation grounds; established the maximum permitted saturation for residential grief expressions. Petitioner subsequently painted the home a Committee-approved pale lavender, which she described as "the color of almost nothing."

**Ruling 38-J, *Petition of Marchetti*:**
Petitioner requested permission to install a small fountain (electric, 18 inches tall) in his front yard as a memorial to his daughter. Approved with conditions (operating hours limited to 7 AM–8 PM, fountain to be winterized November through March). Notable as the first memorial installation approved by the Committee with no time limitation on duration.

## V. INTERNAL MEMORANDUM — NOT FOR PUBLIC DISTRIBUTION

*(Included in this record pursuant to §7.90 — Transparency in Dissent Documentation)*

**TO:** H. Margaux Ferdinandsen, Chair
**FROM:** Carmen Elena Vasquez, Member
**DATE:** December 1
**RE:** Ruling 44-C and the scope of Committee authority

Margaux,

I am writing this because I need it to exist somewhere other than the minutes. I know you will enter it into the record, and I accept that. But I need you to read it first, as yourself, not as the Chair.

Helen Brandt called me after the ruling. She was quiet for a long time and then she said, "My son died in a ditch and they won't let me cry on my own lawn." I did not have an answer for her. I tried to explain our jurisdiction. She listened. Then she said, "You know what I think? I think your Committee is afraid that if people saw me crying, they'd start crying too."

Margaux, I think she's right.

I think we have built a system that is afraid of the sight of human feeling. Not ugly feeling — we permit sadness, in its proper colors, for its permitted duration, with its conditions met. We are not afraid of sadness. We are afraid of *uncontrolled* sadness. We are afraid of the woman on the lawn. We are afraid that if one woman is allowed to kneel in the grass and weep, then everyone will kneel, and the lawns will be full, and no one will be able to walk past without feeling something they did not consent to feel.

But that is what lawns are for, Margaux. That is what the world is for.

I am not resigning. I am staying, because someone on this Committee has to vote for the woman on the grass, and the garden, and the deep plum. But I want you to know — and I say this with affection, because I have liked you, and I have trusted you — that when I read the ruling on Helen Brandt, I felt something I was not given permission to feel. And it did not destroy the neighborhood. It did not exceed any threshold. It was just grief, Margaux. It was just grief, and it was mine, and it was enough.

— Carmen
