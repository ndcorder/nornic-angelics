## Critic Review

The Committee on Elegiac Standards is the portfolio's most sustained act of bureaucratic ventriloquism — a regulatory document so perfectly calibrated in its official register that the dissenting annotations read like oxygen entering a sealed room. The specificity is devastating at every level: "storm of sorrow" appearing seventeen times in the Approved Metaphor Registry because no one could be troubled to write more, the four-hour Silence Window reduced from twelve because people in shock are not easily regulated ("the solution was to regulate them faster"), the 4.7% approval rate for Extended Grief Allocation petitions, Form G-7 asking you to describe your bereavement trajectory using only IMPROVING / STABLE / SLOW DECLINE / RAPID DECLINE / RESOLVED. The annotation escalation is masterfully paced: [1-2] establish the annotator as a pedantic committee insider, [3] introduces the wife's death and the silence that "became a physical thing" the Registry cannot name, [4]'s marginal note ("He was still warm when they handed me the Silence Window notification") is the portfolio's cruelest single sentence, and [10]'s "I used 498. I have two words left. Here they are." is an ending so perfect it feels inevitable rather than written. The complicity mechanism is the portfolio's quietest but most architectural: the reader clicks through ten annotations and in doing so enacts the Committee's own audit process — examining a grieving person's dissent, cataloguing it, moving on. The atmosphere shift (background warming imperceptibly with each annotation read) and the final revelation (three lines appearing after stillness: "It is not registered.") complete the emotional circuit. The CSS escalation — annotation borders darkening from brown through red to near-black, backgrounds warming incrementally — performs visually what the text performs verbally: grief becoming harder to contain. This joins The Posthumous Style Guide, The Severance Package, and The Repudiation Engine as the portfolio's canon of bureaucratic weapons, and it may be the most emotionally devastating: the Committee's language is so precise that its cruelty is deniable, and the annotator's grief is so specific that its denial becomes the final violence.
