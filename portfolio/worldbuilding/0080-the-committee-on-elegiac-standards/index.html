The Committee on Elegiac Standards

Seventh Edition — Effective January 1

Regulatory Framework for Grief Expression
§ This document constitutes the official policy of the Committee. All public and private expressions of grief are subject to its provisions.

Preamble

WHEREAS unregulated grief has been demonstrated to produce cascading inefficiencies in social, economic, and interpersonal systems; and

WHEREAS the Committee has been empowered to ensure that expressions of bereavement adhere to standards of proportionality, temporal appropriateness, and metaphorical clarity;

NOW, THEREFORE, the Committee on Elegiac Standards hereby promulgates the following Regulatory Framework, applicable to all persons, corporate entities, and institutional bodies within its jurisdiction.

[1]

Style note: "cascading inefficiencies" reads awkwardly here. Suggest "disruptive overflow." Also, "empowered" should technically be "constituted and empowered," per the Committee's own founding charter, which no one appears to have read.

Article I — Permissible Modes of Expression

§1.1 General Provisions

All elegiac expressions, whether verbal, written, gestural, or digital, shall conform to the Standards established herein. No expression of grief shall exceed the parameters outlined in Schedule A (Intensity Ratings) without prior Committee authorization.

[2]

Point of order: the cross-reference to Schedule A is incorrect. Schedule A concerns event classification, not intensity ratings. Intensity ratings appear in Schedule C. I have raised this in three consecutive sessions. It has not been corrected.

§1.2 Metaphorical Density

The use of metaphor in elegiac expression is governed by the following limits:

  1. Standard Eloquence Level 1 (bereavement of immediate family member): Maximum of four (4) distinct metaphors per 1,000 words of elegiac text, with no single metaphor exceeding a length of twenty (20) words.
  2. Standard Eloquence Level 2 (bereavement of extended family member, colleague, or acquaintance): Maximum of two (2) metaphors per 1,000 words, each not to exceed twelve (12) words.
  3. Standard Eloquence Level 3 (public figure, pet, abstract loss): Metaphor use is not recommended. If employed, a single metaphor of no more than six (6) words is permitted per 2,500 words.

All metaphors must be drawn from the Committee's Approved Metaphor Registry (Appendix G). Unregistered metaphors constitute a Minor Elegiac Violation subject to the penalties described in §7.3.

[3]

The Registry contains 114 metaphors, most of which involve weather. The phrase "storm of sorrow" appears seventeen times in different subsections, presumably because no one could be troubled to write more. When my wife died I could not find a single entry that described the way silence became a physical thing. The closest was "a quiet rain," which is not what silence does at all.

§1.3 Temporal Constraints

Elegiac expression is subject to mandatory Cooling Periods between acts of grief performance, as follows:

  1. Post-Event Silence Window: A period of not less than four (4) hours nor more than seventy-two (72) hours shall elapse between the bereavement event and the first permitted expression of grief. Expression during the Silence Window constitutes a Severe Elegiac Violation.
  2. Mandatory Remission Interval: Following the conclusion of active elegiac expression, a remission interval of not less than fourteen (14) days shall be observed before subsequent expression is permitted. Petitions for reduced intervals may be filed pursuant to §4.2.
  3. Decay Schedule: All elegiac commitments shall observe the Standard Decay Schedule outlined in Appendix K, which governs the permissible intensity of grief expression over time.
[4]

The four-hour Silence Window was originally twelve hours. It was reduced after the Bentham Review found that people in acute grief "exhibited reduced compliance awareness." A simpler way to say this: the Committee found that people in shock are not easily regulated. The solution was to regulate them faster.

He was still warm when they handed me the Silence Window notification.

Article II — Storage and Transmission

§2.1 Grief Storage

Persons experiencing bereavement are entitled to one (1) Standard Grief Allocation (SGA) per qualifying loss event. An SGA consists of the following:

  1. Private Grief Quota: Up to ninety (90) days of documented private elegiac activity, subject to the temporal and intensity standards of Article I.
  2. Public Grief Quota: Up to three (3) Public Distress Events per SGA, rated and classified according to Schedule A.
  3. Residual Grief Allowance: Following exhaustion of the SGA, a residual allowance of up to twelve (12) months of Low-Intensity Elegiac Activity (Category 1–2 on Schedule C) is available upon petition.

SGAs are non-transferable and non-cumulative. Unused quota does not carry forward.

[5]

"Non-transferable" means you cannot give your grief to anyone else, which is a strange thing to legislate against, since no one has ever managed to do it.

"Non-cumulative" means if you lose two people in one year, you do not get twice as much time. The Committee has determined that grief does not stack. The Committee has determined this from data. The Committee has never held a grieving person and felt the way loss multiplies, compounds, stacks.

§2.2 Transmission Protocols

All public communications of grief shall be routed through Committee-verified channels. Direct transmission of elegiac content via unregulated personal communication is classified as a Grey Elegiac Event and is subject to review.

Verified channels include:

  1. Committee-operated Condolence Portals
  2. Licensed Elegiac Facilitators
  3. Approved institutional memorial services
  4. The Committee's digital bereavement registry
[6]

The point of this section is to ensure grief moves through proper channels, like water through pipes. If grief is not expressed through the pipes it doesn't count. If you stood in a hospital corridor and whispered no no no no no no — not through any verified channel — it didn't happen.

The Committee does not record the sounds people make in corridors.

Article III — Enforcement

§3.1 Classification of Violations

Elegiac violations are classified according to the following schedule:

  1. Minor Elegiac Violation: Exceeding metaphorical density limits; use of unregistered metaphors; failure to observe formatting standards in written elegies.
  2. Standard Elegiac Violation: Exceeding intensity ratings; initiating elegiac expression during the Silence Window; unauthorized public grief performance.
  3. Severe Elegiac Violation: Repeated Standard Violations; operating an unlicensed elegiac facilitation service; grief transmission via unregulated channels resulting in measurable distress to third parties.

Penalties are assessed according to the severity of the violation and the violator's history of elegiac compliance.

[7]

§3.2 Compliance Audits

The Committee reserves the right to conduct random compliance audits of persons holding active SGAs. Audited persons must provide documentation of all elegiac activity, including:

  1. Timestamped records of grief expressions
  2. Metaphor density calculations
  3. Witness attestations for Public Distress Events
  4. A completed Grief Trajectory Assessment (Form G-7)

Failure to comply with an audit constitutes a Severe Elegiac Violation.

[8]

Form G-7 asks you to rate your grief on a scale of 1–10.

It asks this once per week.

It asks you to describe the "trajectory" of your bereavement using only the terms provided:

IMPROVING / STABLE / SLOW DECLINE / RAPID DECLINE / RESOLVED

I don't know what word you use for the morning you wake up and for exactly three seconds you forget they're gone. There is no form for the way that forgetting is its own second loss, a small death on top of the large one, when the remembering comes back.

There is no checkbox for STABLE that means I am holding myself together with my hands and sometimes my hands are not enough.

Article IV — Mandatory Closure

§4.1 Standard Resolution

All SGAs are subject to Mandatory Resolution upon the expiration of the applicable time period. Resolution involves:

  1. A final Grief Trajectory Assessment confirming Category 1 (RESOLVED) status
  2. Submission of a Resolution Statement confirming the bereaved's return to standard emotional baseline
  3. A fourteen (14) day Resolution Quarantine during which no elegiac expression of any kind is permitted

Following successful resolution, the SGA is closed and the bereaved returns to full elegiac compliance.

[9]

You have to sign a form that says you are done.

You have to sign a form that says you are done.

The Resolution Statement has a signature line and a date and the words "I acknowledge that my grief has been processed in accordance with applicable standards."

They want you to sign your name under a sentence that means I felt what I was supposed to feel for as long as I was supposed to feel it and now I am finished.

§4.2 Petitions for Extended Grief

Persons who believe their SGA to be insufficient may petition the Committee for an Extended Grief Allocation (EGA). Petitions must include:

  1. Medical documentation of prolonged bereavement response
  2. A completed Hardship Exemption Application (Form HE-12)
  3. A written statement of no more than 500 words explaining why standard grief has proven insufficient

EGAs are granted at the Committee's sole discretion. Approval rates for the current review period: 4.7%.

[10]
