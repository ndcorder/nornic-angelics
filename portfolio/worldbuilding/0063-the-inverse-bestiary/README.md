## Critic Review

The Inverse Bestiary is the portfolio's most sustained feat of conceptual architecture — thirteen entries, each built on a real perceptual phenomenon, escalating from the saccadic moth's simple temporal hiding place to the certainty flatworm's attack on metacognition itself, culminating in Entry XIII ("The Reader") where the field guide's subject is revealed to have been its reader all along. The specificity is extraordinary throughout: population densities inferred from wing-scale deposits, the 72% forced-choice success rate for the threshold shrew, the optic disc's precise 15-degree temporal eccentricity — each creature's biology is a mirror of a real cognitive blind spot, rendered in Linnaean Latin so convincing it creates its own authority. The diagnostic key is the complicity engine's sharpest version yet: both paths at step 6 lead to confabulation (the carp doesn't need you to be right — it needs you to be confident), and both paths at step 8 lead to certainty (the flatworm doesn't need you to feel certain about everything — just certain about your ability to be uncertain), making the key itself the thirteenth creature's habitat. The confusion index bars filling on scroll, the epigraph from a fictional 1974 symposium, the footer's "Several were present" — every surface serves the thesis. This joins The Posthumous Style Guide, Conjectural Portrait, and The Bureau of Returned Objects as the portfolio's worldbuilding canon, and it may be the most formally perfect of them — a document about the things you cannot perceive that makes your perception of it the final exhibit.
