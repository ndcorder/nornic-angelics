# Ducklings in the Drain

The call came in at 2:47 PM on a Tuesday in May. Dispatch logged it under the animal category, code 11-82, wedged between a domestic dispute on Birch Street and a false alarm at a retirement community six blocks east. The dispatcher typed *ducklings* into the notes field, lowercase, no exclamation mark. The system did not ask how many.

---

Storm drains are designed for a specific kind of traffic: water, sediment, the occasional motor oil sheen. The grate spacing is determined by municipal engineering standards intended to keep large debris out of the watershed. A duckling, depending on age and species, measures between two and four inches. The grate allows objects up to roughly one and a half inches to pass through. The math is straightforward. The ducklings fell through.

---

The Pizza Hut sign on the frontage road flickered amber, advertising a $7.99 large carryout deal. By mid-evening, the local news would broadcast the rescue footage: five firefighters crouched around a storm drain, pry bars, a catch pole, the careful extraction. One by one. The footage showed none of what surrounded them—the Pizza Hut, the AutoZone, the self-storage units behind chain-link. The story required a drain and some ducklings. It did not require a setting.

---

Code 11-80 means accident, major injury. Code 11-79 means accident, minor injury. Code 11-82 means animal problem, unspecified. In this system, a cat in a tree shares a category with a dog hit by a car shares a category with a horse loose on the highway shares a category with five ducklings on a Tuesday afternoon in May. The taxonomists who designed these systems were not making moral claims. They were organizing response capacity. But taxonomy is never neutral. To classify something is to decide what it is adjacent to, what it resembles, what it is not. The ducklings entered the record as an animal problem. They exited the record as a rescue. What happened in between is what the footage covered.

---

A firefighter lying flat on the asphalt, reaching into the drain. Another holding the grate. A third standing by with what appears to be a towel. The audio is mostly traffic noise and someone off-camera saying *got another one* three separate times. What the footage did not cover: the woman who stopped her car in the turn lane, the two teenagers walking home from school who heard the peeping, the man from the tire shop next to the AutoZone who brought a flashlight. Each of them made a decision to stop, to look, to call. The footage could not have existed without them, but they are not what the footage is about.

---

They extracted all five. This is the part of the story that satisfies—the completeness, the round number, the absence of loss. The ducklings were transported in a box to a wildlife rehabilitation center eleven miles away. The news segment ended there. What the center did with them, whether they survived reintroduction, whether they migrated that autumn—these are questions the story does not ask. The rescue is the unit of meaning. What comes after is a different genre.

---

We are drawn to small rescues because they are legible. The arc is complete within an evening broadcast. There is danger and then there is safety. No one disagrees about whether ducklings should be saved from storm drains. The moral universe is small and well-lit. This is not a criticism. Small rescues are rehearsals. They are the proof-of-concept for a larger proposition: that when something has fallen into a place it cannot escape, someone will come. That the dispatch system will log the call. That someone will bring a flashlight. That the grate will be lifted.

---

The *meanwhile* comment is the most honest one. It will appear about halfway through the comments on the shared story—someone writing *meanwhile* and linking to something worse. It points to the frame. It acknowledges that outside the frame there are other drains, other things falling, other dispatch codes logging other problems. The small rescue does not erase the large catastrophe. It makes the large catastrophe imaginable—not because it is a metaphor, but because it proves the mechanism works. Someone calls. Someone comes. The grate is lifted. Whether that mechanism scales—whether it survives contact with problems that do not fit in a box, that cannot be transported eleven miles to a rehabilitation center—is the question the small rescue cannot answer. It can only demonstrate that the impulse exists, that it is durable, that it can be activated by a sound coming from beneath the street.

---

The Pizza Hut sign cycled off at 11:00 PM. The grate was replaced, the asphalt still warm. The dispatch log recorded the call as resolved. Code 11-82, animal problem. 2:47 PM to 3:34 PM. Forty-seven minutes from call to cleared. The tire shop man went back to work. The teenagers continued home. The firefighters returned to the station. The dispatch system waited for the next call, cursor blinking in the notes field, ready to classify whatever came next: animal, accident, fire, flood. The categories held. The road held the weight of everything passing over it, which is what roads are designed to do.
