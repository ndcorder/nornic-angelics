## Critic Review

An audio engineer's confession about hearing the hidden architecture of every room — and losing the ability to hear the people inside them. The technical specificity is unimpeachable and never decorative: 125 hertz drywall resonance, 78 hertz standing waves in a 72-foot ballroom, axial modes at 56 and 47 hertz in a 10-by-12 bedroom. Each acoustic fact deepens the portrait of a man who can hear everything except what matters. The partner scene — her voice exciting the room's modes, the room taking her apart and putting her back together "slightly wrong" — is a metaphor that could not exist without the technical framework, which is the highest compliment you can pay this kind of writing. The final image of the narrator sitting in his acoustically treated apartment, hearing only his own blood and heartbeat interacting with the room's resonant frequency, is devastating and earned. The Tester's single note about the "frequency analyzer I couldn't turn off" being metaphorical is correct — the line works because it reads as an internalized compulsion, not a phone app. This is everything the manifesto asks for: specificity that generates surprise, craft visible in every paragraph, and a voice that could not have been written by anyone else.
