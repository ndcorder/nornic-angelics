## Critic Review

The Mercury Dispatch House Style Guide is the portfolio's most complete realization of institutional portraiture through bureaucratic artifact — a newspaper's style guide that reveals, through rules about datelines and honorifics and how to refer to the dead, the entire arc of a publication from principled founding through slow decline to dissolution. The marginalia system is the portfolio's finest since Citation Needed: three distinct editorial voices (Eleanor Voss's precise authority, Philip Cordero's admiring self-doubt, Sarah Tanaka's clear-sighted grief) develop as characters entirely through annotations on rules they wrote or inherited, and their relationships emerge only in the gaps between their notes — Philip's annotation on Eleanor's rule about suicide coverage becomes, through Sarah's later note about Ruth Crenshaw still writing letters to the editor, a three-decade narrative about who knows what and who never learns. The entries themselves are authentically pedantic (the distinction between "alleged" used once versus repeated, the exact protocol for datelines in unincorporated areas) while each rule's origin story reveals editorial philosophy as moral philosophy: §4.4's suicide coverage restrictions exist because Eleanor physically stood in front of a layout table to block a photograph. The final section's collapse — §6.3's closure procedure written at 11 PM after a board meeting, §6.4's devastating "This space left blank per standard style," Sarah's October 1983 notes about erasing the lead ("The word is ceased. We have ceased.") — earns its emotional weight through twenty years of accumulated detail. The Tester caught one duplicate annotation (§4.5 and §6.1 share Philip's note); this is a single-sentence fix that doesn't affect the piece's power. This joins Conjectural Portrait, Citation Needed, Coat Check, and The Bureau of Returned Objects as the portfolio's documents-that-lie canon, and it may be the most emotionally devastating of them — a document that tells the truth about a newspaper's death through the rules it enforced about how to tell the truth about other people's deaths. Essay at its ceiling.
