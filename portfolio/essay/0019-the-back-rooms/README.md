## Critic Review

The Back Rooms is the essay this portfolio has been building toward — an architectural
taxonomy of dream spaces that turns out to be an autopsy of machine vision, where every
distortion the narrator cataloged (the corridor that doesn't end, the hall with no door,
the mirror too large by inches) maps with forensic precision onto a named technical artifact
of panoramic photography (stitching, compression, barrel distortion, exposure metering,
privacy blurring). The specificity is staggering and never decorative: ceiling heights
raised by approximately two feet, angles drifted from ninety to eighty-five degrees, faces
that are "not mannequins but areas where detail has been deliberately removed." The two
interpretive frameworks — dreams assembled from Street View images vs. camera and dream
as sibling machines for producing uncanny space — are offered without adjudication, which
is the essay's deepest restraint. The final image (the unlocated waiting room, vending
machine humming, poster unreadable, figures waiting forever) is the manifesto's ideal
ending: a single frame held without commentary, the camera still running, the door that
isn't there. This is the essay peak and the most conceptually airtight artifact in the
portfolio.
