The kitchen in my mother's house has yellow walls. This is a fact I can verify against photographs, against the memory of sitting at the table doing homework while she cooked, against the way certain afternoons turned the wallpaper the color of a bruise. But in the version I revisit between three and four a.m., the walls are something closer to ivory, and the window above the sink faces a different direction. The floor plan is the same—galley-style, refrigerator to the left of the stove, the hallway leading past the bathroom toward the bedrooms—but it has been widened by approximately two feet, and the ceiling has been raised. Not enough to look wrong. Enough to make the proportions feel like someone describing the room from notes rather than from presence.

I have been here many times.

The hotel corridor dates from my late twenties, though I cannot tell you which hotel or which city. It has the universal quality of such places—intermittent lighting, a carpet pattern designed to disguise stains, doors that recede toward a vanishing point that the corridor never quite reaches. In the dream, I walk this hallway for what feels like twenty minutes. The doors are numbered but the numbers repeat. The exit signs glow their characteristic red but they illuminate nothing. When I pause at any particular door and open it, the room inside is always the same room: a bed with a white duvet, a window facing a brick wall, a television mounted too high. Every room the same room. Every door the same door. The corridor continues.

There is an exam hall that appears perhaps twice a year. University-style: tiered seating, a clock mounted at the front that I can never read, the acoustics of a space designed to make whispered questions into public events. The room has no door. I mean this literally—I have traced the walls and there is no point of egress. The students around me are writing with furious concentration but when I glance at their papers, the pages are blank. The invigilator is a woman with her back turned, gazing out a window at a parking lot that belongs to a building I do not recognize. She never turns around.

The bathroom from childhood has a mirror that is slightly too large, extending past the point where the real mirror ended. The reflection shows the correct room but the geometry is wrong—angles that should be ninety degrees have drifted to something like eighty-five, and the face I see is mine but not mine, the way a map is not the territory but a useful enough fiction for navigation.

I started cataloging these rooms three years ago, thinking they might have something to tell me about architecture, memory, the mind's capacity for construction. The catalog now runs to forty-seven entries. Each one described in terms of square footage, lighting conditions, dominant colors, the position of windows relative to doorways. Each one cross-referenced against the others to identify recurring features: the tendency for ceilings to be higher than they should be, for corridors to extend beyond their real-world terminus, for faces to be absent or unreadable in spaces that should contain people.

The method was simple. I returned to the addresses. Not in person—the distances were wrong, the years were wrong—but through the screen, using the application that lets you visit any street in the mapped world without leaving your chair. My mother's house in Ohio. The hotel in Indianapolis where I once stayed for a conference, whose corridor I walked at two in the morning because I could not sleep. The university in Michigan. The hospital in Toledo where my grandmother spent her final week. I found the addresses, or what I believed to be the addresses, and I moved through them the way you move through those places: by clicking, by dragging the view, by rotating through three hundred and sixty degrees of captured light.

Here is what I found.

In the kitchen of my mother's house, as rendered by the camera in the summer of 2019, the walls are ivory. The original image has yellowed in my memory; Street View remembers the room as it was before age entered the walls. The window above the sink faces the direction the dream insists it faces. I had been remembering it wrong for twenty years, and the dream had been remembering it right.

The hotel corridor in Indianapolis does not end. This is not a metaphor. The panoramic camera captures the hallway as a continuous stitched plane, and the stitching extends the visual field past the point where the physical corridor terminates in a fire door. The door is there in the source photographs—you can see it if you know to look—but the panoramic wrap folds it into itself, and what you experience is a hallway that curves past the limits of the building. The repeating door numbers are an artifact of compression. The doors that open onto the same room: an artifact of the format's inability to render what lies behind each successive point of interest, so that clicking through successive doors returns you, always, to the same stored interior.

The exam hall has no door because the camera entered through it and then the door closed, and the panorama stitched the wall closed behind the point of entry. You can see the seam if you look for it—a thin vertical line where the pattern of the wallpaper does not quite match. The invigilator with her back turned is a face that the algorithm has blurred for privacy, and the blur has been shaped by the software into something that looks like a woman staring out a window, because the human mind cannot tolerate an absence of narrative and will construct a figure from any collection of pixels that suggests one.

The bathroom mirror is too large because the reflective surface confused the exposure metering, and the HDR algorithm expanded the bright area to compensate, stretching the mirror past its physical frame. The angles that should be ninety degrees have drifted because the panoramic stitch has introduced barrel distortion—a slight curving of straight lines that the software is supposed to correct but does not always manage. The face in the mirror is my face but wrong because the camera that captured it was a machine photographing a reflective surface at an angle, and what it recorded was a face blurred and resampled until it became a generic approximation of a human visage.

There are two ways to understand this. The first is that the rooms I visit in sleep are not memories at all but reconstructions assembled from images I absorbed while awake, scrolling through locations at random, letting the particular quality of Street View's unreality imprint itself on my visual cortex until my dreaming mind could reproduce it with architectural precision. The dreams are not about the places. They are about the photographs of the places.

The second is that the camera and the dream are both machines for the production of uncanny space, and that they converge on the same distortions because they share a common architecture. Both flatten three dimensions into a continuous surface. Both struggle with faces. Both produce light without a source, spaces without exits, corridors that extend beyond their physical limits. Both exist in a temporal present that is never quite now—the dream because it has already happened by the time you try to describe it, the photograph because it is always a record of a moment that is definitively over.

Last night I visited a room I had not cataloged before. A waiting area of some kind—molded plastic chairs, fluorescent lighting, a vending machine against the far wall. The vending machine was stocked but the items behind the glass were too bright, their colors saturated past the point of realism, as though the image had been adjusted to compensate for the artificial interior lighting. Two figures sat in the plastic chairs, but their faces were smooth and featureless, not in the way of mannequins but in the way of areas where detail has been deliberately removed. The floor was a composite tile, gray and beige, the kind designed for institutional traffic. There was a poster on the wall but I could not read it—the text had been reduced to a pattern of horizontal lines.

I woke and reached for the notebook I keep on the nightstand. I wrote: *Waiting room. Plastic chairs. Vending machine colors wrong. Faces blurred. Floor tile gray-beige. Poster unreadable. No windows. No door visible.*

This morning I searched for the room. I have not found it yet. But I know it is there, archived somewhere in the vast library of photographed interiors, waiting for me to find the right address, the right sequence of clicks that will carry me through a door I cannot see into a room I have already visited in the only way that matters—a room that exists because a camera saw it once, and because my mind, presented with the camera's particular failures of representation, accepted them as the grammar of a space more real than reality, and has been speaking that grammar ever since, building rooms I have never entered from light that never fell, and furnishing them with everything the algorithm forgets to include when it is trying to remember what the world looks like.

The vending machine hums. The figures wait. The poster on the wall says something I cannot quite read.
