—ArmenG (talk · contribs) 08:32, 14 January 2033 (UTC)

The "Karkan Central Health Authority" is not a recognized medical body—it's a political entity operating within the breakaway region. Per WP:RS and WP:MEDRS, we should not be citing health statistics from a contested authority. The Eurasian Institute is a well-established think tank with a track record of independent analysis.

—CaspianObserver (talk) 09:17, 14 January 2033 (UTC)

That's a remarkable claim. The KCHA operates the only hospital network in the region. Are you arguing that the hospital records maintained by the doctors treating the wounded are inherently unreliable? The bias here is transparent.

I'm arguing that a body established by a separatist administration has a vested interest in inflating casualty numbers for international sympathy. I note that you've made 47 edits to this article and related pages, and every single one introduces language favorable to the Karkan side. You might want to review WP:COI and WP:NPOV.

—CaspianObserver (talk) 10:45, 14 January 2033 (UTC)

Please assume good faith and do not make personal attacks. My interest in this article stems from family connections to the region. I have disclosed this on my user page. If you have evidence of COI, take it to WP:COIN. Otherwise, engage with the sources. The KCHA figures are reported by Reuters, Al Jazeera, and Médecins Sans Frontières. 1.2 Request for comment: Casualty figures

Question: Should the article include casualty figures from the Karkan Central Health Authority alongside those from the Eurasian Institute of Conflict Studies? If so, how should the discrepancy between sources be presented?

—Validatore (talk) 11:00, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

Yes, include both with attribution. The article should present the range of credible estimates with clear attribution. The infobox should show "estimates range from 8,000–14,000 (Eurasian Institute) to 22,000+ (KCHA, as reported by Reuters and MSF)." The body should explain the methodological differences: the EI figure represents "confirmed" casualties based on cross-referenced reports, while the KCHA figure includes "presumed" casualties based on hospital intake records and family reports. Both approaches have merits and limitations. Excluding either paints a misleading picture.

—DorothyInOz (talk) 12:30, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

Qualified yes. I support including the KCHA figures with appropriate caveats about the source's political context. Per WP:DUE, we should weight the Eurasian Institute figures more heavily as they represent the majority view in international reporting, but the KCHA figures are notable enough for inclusion, particularly given their citation by major outlets.

—Validatore (talk) 13:15, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

No. The KCHA is an arm of a political entity. Its casualty figures have not undergone independent verification. Including them gives them a veneer of legitimacy they have not earned. Per WP:MEDRS, medical claims should be sourced to peer-reviewed literature, not hospital press releases from a war zone.

—CaspianObserver (talk) 14:50, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

WP:MEDRS applies to medical and health claims—treatment efficacy, disease information, etc. Casualty statistics are not medical claims in that sense; they're demographic data. The relevant policy is WP:RS, and under RS, the KCHA figures as reported by Reuters and MSF clearly meet the threshold for inclusion. You're invoking the wrong policy.

—DorothyInOz (talk) 16:10, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

Agree with DorothyInOz. I would also note that WP:RS specifically addresses the use of local sources in conflict zones and states that "local health authorities are generally considered reliable for casualty figures even in contested regions, provided their figures are corroborated by independent outlets." Reuters and MSF corroboration clearly satisfies this standard.

—LatishaMK (talk · contribs) 23:05, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

Comment: I'd just like to note that this "dispute" is entirely manufactured. In most conflict articles—Syria, Yemen, Ukraine—we present casualty figures from multiple sources without this level of hand-wringing about which authority is "legitimate." The only reason this is contentious is because one side's government has invested in shaping the Wikipedia narrative. I'll leave it to others to connect the dots.

—LatishaMK (talk · contribs) 23:05, 17 January 2033 (UTC)

Yes, include with attribution. Include both. Present the range. Note the sources. Trust readers to think for themselves. This is what an encyclopedia is supposed to do.

—ArmenG (talk · contribs) 08:00, 18 January 2033 (UTC)

Include both, but weight appropriately. Per WP:GEVAL, we don't give equal validity to all claims. The structure should be: lead with the most widely cited estimate (Eurasian Institute), then note the higher estimate from local health authorities with attribution. Something like: "Estimated casualties range from 8,000–14,000 (Eurasian Institute) to over 22,000 (Karkan Central Health Authority)."

—Dr. Strangechord (talk) admin 10:30, 19 January 2033 (UTC)

Consensus: Include both estimates with clear attribution and methodological context.

There is clear community consensus to include the KCHA figures alongside the Eurasian Institute figures. The relevant policy is WP:RS, not WP:MEDRS, and the KCHA figures as corroborated by Reuters, Al Jazeera, and MSF meet the reliable source threshold. The article should present both estimates with attribution, noting the methodological differences between "confirmed" and "presumed" counts. CaspianObserver's concern about source reliability has been noted but does not override the weight of community opinion and policy interpretation.

—Dr. Strangechord (talk) admin 11:00, 25 January 2033 (UTC)

2 NPOV language: "massacre" vs. "incident"

The article currently describes the events at Hadrut as a "massacre" in the section heading. The term is loaded and emotive. Wikipedia's WP:NPOV policy requires neutral language. I propose renaming the section to "Hadrut incident" or "Hadrut civilian casualties."

—CaspianObserver (talk) 09:00, 2 March 2033 (UTC)

Reuters, BBC, The New York Times, Human Rights Watch, and the UN Independent International Commission of Inquiry on the Karkan Region have all used the word "massacre" in their reporting on Hadrut. The term is not an editorial judgment by Wikipedia editors—it is the consensus description of reliable sources. Per WP:RS/AC, when reliable sources consistently use a term, we should follow that usage.

—DorothyInOz (talk) 09:45, 2 March 2033 (UTC)

You're cherry-picking. The Zanadu Ministry of Foreign Affairs describes it as a "counter-terrorism operation with civilian casualties." The Baku Policy Forum uses "Hadrut incident." There are reliable sources on both sides of this question. Per WP:NPOV, we should use the most neutral term, which is "incident."

—CaspianObserver (talk) 10:20, 2 March 2033 (UTC)

With respect, you are comparing the reports of independent international organizations and major global news outlets with the statements of a government that is a party to the conflict. The Zanadu MFA is not a reliable, independent source for this purpose—it is an involved party making self-serving statements. The Baku Policy Forum, as has been discussed on this page before, receives substantial funding from Zanadu state interests. This is the equivalent of citing the Russian MFA's descriptions of Bucha. Surely you see the problem.

—LatishaMK (talk · contribs) 02:14, 3 March 2033 (UTC)

The notion that any source from Zanadu or its allies is inherently unreliable while any source from Karkan-aligned entities is presumptively credible is itself a violation of NPOV. I'm not going to engage with this double standard further.

—CaspianObserver (talk) 11:00, 3 March 2033 (UTC)

Then I'll note for the record that you have declined to address the substance of the argument about independent vs. involved-party sources. The community will draw its own conclusions.

—LatishaMK (talk · contribs) 02:45, 4 March 2033 (UTC)

"Hadrut massacre" should redirect here regardless of the section heading. — GeologyOfRivers (talk) 04:11, 4 March 2033 (UTC)

I've reviewed the sources cited by both sides. The count is: 9 independent international sources using "massacre," 2 government-affiliated sources using "incident," and 3 sources using neutral phrasing like "killings" or "deaths." Per WP:COMMONNAME, the article should reflect the usage most common in reliable sources, which is clearly "massacre." I've updated the section heading accordingly. If someone wants to add a footnote noting the Zanadu government's preferred terminology, that would be appropriate for balance.

—Validatore (talk) 16:00, 5 March 2033 (UTC)

4 Page name and scope

Should the page be titled "Karkan Conflict" or "Karkan War"? Most international sources use "conflict," but the scale of the fighting (two sovereign militaries, armored engagements, aerial bombardment, 22,000+ casualties) would typically qualify as a war under most definitions. Thoughts?

—Validatore (talk) 10:00, 20 July 2033 (UTC)

Per WP:COMMONNAME, the article title should reflect the name most commonly used in reliable English-language sources. A Google News search shows "Karkan Conflict" outnumbering "Karkan War" by roughly 4:1. The current title is appropriate.

—DorothyInOz (talk) 11:30, 20 July 2033 (UTC)

"Conflict" is also the term preferred by the Zanadu government, which deliberately avoids "war" because declaring war would trigger certain treaty obligations. The terminology itself is politically loaded, and Wikipedia's reliance on "common usage" effectively adopts the framing of the more powerful party.

—ArmenG (talk · contribs) 12:15, 20 July 2033 (UTC)

That's an interesting point, ArmenG, but WP:COMMONNAME doesn't ask us to adjudicate political framing—it asks us to follow the sources. If you can demonstrate that "Karkan War" is gaining traction in reliable sources, the title can be revisited. Right now, the sources say "conflict."

—Validatore (talk) 13:00, 20 July 2033 (UTC)

I'd also like to raise the question of the date range. "(2031–2033)" implies the conflict has ended or will end in 2033. As of July 2033, the conflict is ongoing, with active combat reported as recently as last week. Should the end date be removed until there's a formal ceasefire or peace agreement?

—LatishaMK (talk · contribs) 02:30, 21 July 2033 (UTC)

Good catch. I've moved the page to "Karkan Conflict" without the end date. We can add "(2031–2033)" if and when a formal resolution occurs. — Dr. Strangechord admin 09:00, 21 July 2033 (UTC)

5 Deletion review (October 2033)

⚠ This section discusses the deletion review for this article. The full discussion can be found at Wikipedia:Deletion review/Log/2033 October 15. A summary is preserved below.
The article was deleted on 12 October 2033 following an Articles for deletion discussion. This section documents the subsequent deletion review.

On 12 October 2033, the article Karkan Conflict was deleted after an AfD discussion that closed as "delete." The closing admin, NeutralHaus, determined there was consensus that the topic failed WP:GNG (General Notability Guideline). The deletion review was opened on 15 October 2033.

Summary of original AfD discussion — 8–12 October 2033 (click to expand) [show]

Delete

WP:GNG requires significant coverage in reliable, independent sources. While the Karkan Conflict has received media attention, the vast majority of coverage is from advocacy organizations and regional outlets with a direct stake in the conflict's framing. Independent international coverage—by which I mean coverage in outlets with no regional affiliation—is limited. The New York Times ran a 400-word wire summary in August 2031 and has not revisited the topic. The Guardian published one long-form piece in March 2032. Compare this to the coverage of comparable conflicts, which receive sustained, in-depth reporting from multiple independent outlets. This topic, while tragic, does not meet the threshold.

—ConsensusBuilder99 (talk) 10:00, 9 October 2033 (UTC)

The conflict received sustained coverage from Al Jazeera, Deutsche Welle, BBC, Le Monde, and The Economist. Is the argument that none of these qualify as independent international sources?

—DorothyInOz (talk) 11:30, 9 October 2033 (UTC)

Al Jazeera and Deutsche Welle both receive state funding from governments that have taken diplomatic positions on this conflict. That's not "independent" in the sense WP:GNG intends. BBC and Le Monde coverage was limited. The Economist mentioned it in a "briefing" column, not a cover story. We need to distinguish between some coverage and sustained, significant coverage.

—ConsensusBuilder99 (talk) 12:15, 9 October 2033 (UTC)

This is a conflict that killed tens of thousands of people, displaced hundreds of thousands more, involved two sovereign states, and was the subject of a UN Commission of Inquiry, three UN Security Council sessions, and an International Court of Justice case. If that does not meet our notability threshold, then our notability threshold is broken.

—LatishaMK (talk · contribs) 23:45, 9 October 2033 (UTC)

Deletion review close

Result: Consensus to overturn deletion and restore the article

After careful review, I am overturning the AfD close and restoring the article. The original AfD close was flawed in several respects:

  1. The closing admin improperly discounted policy-based "keep" arguments as "emotional," when several keep !votes cited specific policies and sources in detail.
  2. The standard of "truly independent" applied by delete !voters has no basis in existing policy or guideline. Under current policy, the BBC, Al Jazeera, Reuters, and other cited outlets clearly qualify as reliable, independent sources.
  3. The volume and depth of sourcing—47 sources including multiple long-form pieces from international outlets, two human rights organization reports, a UN Commission of Inquiry report, an ICJ case, and academic literature—clearly meets WP:GNG by any reasonable interpretation.
  4. The documented history of sockpuppetry and coordinated editing by at least one "delete" !voter undermines the integrity of the original AfD discussion.

The article has been restored and moved to Karkan Conflict (2031–2033), reflecting the formal ceasefire signed on 28 September 2033. I am also imposing a temporary restriction: no further AfD nominations of this article for a period of 12 months without prior approval from ArbCom.
