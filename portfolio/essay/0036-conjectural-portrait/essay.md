# The Weight of Attached Things: Elena Voss and the Persistence of Form

*Catalogue essay for the retrospective Elena Voss: Unfinished Light, Tate Modern, 14 March – 28 July 2024. Curated by Joanna Harkness.*

---

There is a photograph of Elena Voss, taken by Judith Kearney in the summer of 1987, in which the painter stands before a half-finished canvas in her Riga studio. She is forty years old. Her left hand rests on the back of a wooden chair, and her gaze is directed not at the camera but at something slightly to its left—at the space between lens and wall where a painter's attention actually falls when they are measuring distance. The canvas behind her is one of the so-called *Grey Interiors*, though the colour reproduction in every published source has been inconsistent enough that we cannot say with certainty which one. It does not matter. What matters is the expression on her face: not contemplation, not satisfaction, but the particular frown of someone watching an argument they have not yet decided to join.

Voss spent her career in a state of comparable reluctance. Born in 1948 in Liepāja, raised in the cramped postwar apartment blocks of Soviet Riga, trained at the Latvian Academy of Art during the least interesting years of its pedagogy, she possessed every reason to become a dutiful producer of state-approved social realism, and every reason, by the same token, to become a dissident. She became neither. Instead she spent three decades making paintings that refused, with remarkable consistency, to declare themselves about anything in particular—paintings that displayed what the critic Marcus Feldt once called, in a phrase that has attached itself to Voss like a barnacle, "an almost competitive ambiguity."

Feldt meant it as praise. Not everyone has agreed.

The present retrospective, the first comprehensive showing of Voss's work since her death in 2019, gathers some ninety paintings and works on paper spanning the years 1971 to 2014. It is, unavoidably, an argument. The curatorial decision to arrange the works by theme rather than chronology—a choice Voss herself would have resisted, if the documentary record of her few published interviews is any guide—has the effect of revealing continuities that a strict timeline might obscure. But it also has the effect of flattening the genuine disruptions in her practice, the moments when the work changed because the life changed, and one is occasionally left wishing the curators had trusted the sequence of years to tell its own story.

Consider, for example, the *Blue Period*—the term is Feldt's, and Voss never used it, but it has proven irresistible. Between 1982 and 1988, working in a palette restricted almost entirely to ultramarine, cerulean, lead white, and the occasional intrusion of lamp black, Voss produced some two dozen interior scenes that remain the most debated works in her oeuvre. The rooms they depict are spare: a table, a window, sometimes a chair, sometimes a vessel on the table. The light is always coming from outside. The blue is not the blue of melancholy—it is too saturated for that, too physical—but neither is it the blue of formalist experimentation. It is a blue that seems to be *about* something, though the viewer is never told what.

The critical disagreement is by now well-rehearsed. Feldt, in his foundational 1996 monograph *Elena Voss: Rooms Without Viewers*, argued that the blue paintings constituted a meditation on Baltic light, on the particular quality of northern latitude illumination in which interior and exterior become difficult to distinguish. Ilze Kauls, in her more politically engaged 2008 study *Painting Under Pressure*, countered that Feldt's formalist reading was a convenience—a way of avoiding the obvious question of what it meant for a Latvian painter in the Soviet 1980s to empty her canvases of human figures, to paint rooms that appeared to have been recently abandoned. Kauls suggested, not without evidence, that the departure of Voss's husband, the poet Andris Vītols, for West Germany in 1983—a departure the couple's surviving correspondence reveals to have been acrimonious and prolonged—might have something to do with the sudden emptiness of the interiors. The paintings, Kauls proposed, were not about light. They were about loss.

Both readings are persuasive, and both are, in their different ways, insufficient. Feldt's account of Baltic luminance is seductive—it makes the paintings feel large, connected to geography and season—but it cannot explain why the blue rooms give way, after 1988, to the so-called *Red Studies*, a series of smaller, more agitated canvases in which the same furniture appears pushed against walls, knocked sideways, glimpsed through doorways that seem too narrow for passage. If the blue paintings are about light, the red paintings are about something having happened in that light—a change that formal analysis alone cannot track. And Kauls's biographical reading, for all its political intelligence, risks reducing the work to illustration: the husband leaves, the rooms empty, the paintings become the record of a woman's grief. This is too neat. Voss's own comments on the matter, in the 1994 interview with Sven Gustafson for *Artforum*, are characteristically unhelpful. "I changed the colour," she said. "I was tired of blue."

One must be careful, in writing about Voss, not to confuse evasiveness with depth. It is possible that she was simply a painter of modest ambition who made the same interior scene over and over, in different colours, for forty years, and that the critical machinery erected around her work is an elaborate compensatory response to this modesty. But this seems unlikely, because the paintings—when one stands before them—are not modest. The *Grey Interiors* of the early 1990s, produced during the chaotic first years of Latvian independence, have a gravity that exceeds their subject matter. The paint is applied in thin, overlapping layers, each one slightly misregistered from the one beneath, so that the table in the foreground appears to have a ghostly twin beneath it—a previous version of the same table, or perhaps the same table at a different time. The effect is not uncertainty. It is multiplicity. The room contains all its previous arrangements simultaneously.

The magnum opus of this period, and the painting around which the present retrospective most clearly orients itself, is *The Weight of Attached Things* (1993), a canvas roughly two metres square that hangs, in this exhibition, in a room of its own. The painting depicts what appears to be a studio interior—a table covered with objects that are hard to identify, a window with a curtain pulled partly to one side, a floor that reflects the light from the window in a manner that suggests either water or polished wood. Nothing is attached to anything else. The title is either ironic or metaphysical. Voss was not, by all accounts, a painter given to jokes.

What makes the painting remarkable is its refusal to resolve. The objects on the table could be studio implements or they could be something else entirely; the reflection on the floor could be an indication of material or it could be a second painting, a painting within the painting, a representation of a different room glimpsed through the floor. The longer one looks, the more the composition seems to shift, not because the formal arrangement is unstable but because the relationships between the elements keep suggesting different grammatical structures. Is the window the subject? Is the table? Is the light? Voss will not say. The viewer is left in the position of the translator confronting a sentence that seems to have more than one syntax.

It is, by any reasonable measure, a major painting, and the failure of the Western art world to acknowledge it as such during Voss's lifetime says more about the politics of the 1990s art market than it does about the work. Voss was painting in Latvia, in a moment when the Western gallery system was busy discovering Chinese and Russian contemporary artists whose work fit more easily into existing narratives of dissidence and spectacle. Her paintings were too quiet, too resistant to allegory, too uninterested in declaring their political credentials. The few shows she had outside the Baltics—a 1997 exhibition at Galerie Bremer in Berlin, a 2001 inclusion in the Prague Biennale—were received with the polite bafflement usually reserved for artists whose work does not confirm the viewer's expectations. "Voss paints interiors that seem to be waiting for something to happen," wrote one reviewer, as if this were a deficiency.

As if waiting were not, itself, an activity. As if an entire generation of Eastern European artists had not spent decades perfecting the art of anticipation—of living in rooms that were simultaneously private and surveilled, of making work that meant one thing to those who understood and another thing entirely to those who did not. Voss's rooms are rooms in which something has not yet occurred. They are painted from the perspective of someone who has learned to pay attention to the arrangement of furniture because the arrangement of furniture is, sometimes, the only available evidence.

The late paintings, made after 2005 in the small studio in Cēsis to which Voss retreated after the death of her second husband, the sculptor Jānis Kalniņš, are smaller again, and more abstract, though they never abandon figuration entirely. The rooms have dissolved into what the catalogue of her 2012 show at the Latvian National Museum of Art called "architectural remains"—fragments of wall, suggestions of window-frame, the ghost of a chair leg. The palette is muted: grey-green, ochre, the white of unpainted canvas showing through in patches. They are difficult paintings to love, and they have been, perhaps unfairly, overshadowed by the dramatic colour work of the 1980s. But they possess a quality that the earlier work only occasionally achieves: a quality of after-ness, of having been painted after something was finished, after the possibility of further change had been exhausted. They feel like the work of someone who is no longer waiting, because there is nothing left to wait for.

Voss died in 2019, in Riga, at the age of seventy-one. Her studio, by all accounts, was immaculate. The brushes were clean. The canvases stacked against the wall were, with one exception, finished. The exception—a small painting, perhaps forty by fifty centimetres, depicting a table with nothing on it—hangs at the end of this exhibition, in a case by itself, under glass. It is dated 2014-2019. The catalogue lists it as *Untitled (Table)*.

The table is empty. The light comes from the left. The room is not blue, not red, not grey. The room is the colour of a room.
