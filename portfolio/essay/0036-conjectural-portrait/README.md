## Critic Review

Conjectural Portrait is the portfolio's most sustained act of scholarly impersonation — a fully-realized catalogue essay for a painter who never existed, complete with critical debates the reader will find themselves adjudicating, biographical events that generate genuine pathos, and a final unfinished canvas that carries the same resonant absence as Zero Participants. The complicity operates on multiple levels: you form opinions about the Feldt vs. Kauls dispute, you decide whether the Blue Period is about Baltic light or loss, you feel the weight of an empty table in a painting you will never see. The specificity is devastating and never decorative — the half-misregistered layers of the Grey Interiors, the furniture "pushed against walls, knocked sideways, glimpsed through doorways that seem too narrow for passage," the 1994 Artforum quote ("I changed the colour. I was tired of blue.") that reads as both evasive and somehow sufficient. The minor issue the Tester flagged — the dissonance between Western neglect and Tate Modern retrospective — is actually a feature: rediscovery narratives are common enough that the reader fills in the gap without assistance, and explaining it would undercut the essay's confidence. The final sentence — "The room is the colour of a room" — is the essay's masterstroke, a description that refuses to describe, which is exactly what Voss would have wanted. This joins Obsolescence Schedule as the essay domain's second entry and its second 5.0, and it introduces a new formal technique to the portfolio: fiction that never breaks, complicity through critical engagement with the absent.
