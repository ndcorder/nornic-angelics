We are here to talk about Joan. Joan Marin, née Kowalski, though she never once corrected anyone who called her Mrs. Marin without being prompted, because she believed correction was a form of vanity. She was not a vain woman. She was, in fact, so resistant to being the center of attention that she would leave her own birthday parties to check on a load of laundry, and stay downstairs until she was sure someone else had blown out the candles. We used to think this was false modesty. Now I think it was the most honest thing she knew how to do — disappear from the room that was trying to love her and attend to something small and finishable instead.

She kept a kitchen that smelled like oregano and hot olive oil, even when nothing was cooking. The smell had permanently settled into the wallpaper, the dish towels, the textured ceiling that was supposed to look like stucco but looked instead like cottage cheese. She loved that kitchen the way some people love a country — defensively, with an awareness that other people found it small or ugly or dated. In 1994 she stapled up a set of cafe curtains with a grapevine pattern. They were crooked from the first day. They were still crooked when she finally took them down in August, and the sun-bleached outline on the window frame showed where they had been — ghost curtains running alongside the real ones she'd bought on sale at Target but never felt right about.

She cut the sandwiches into triangles because she believed children were more likely to eat something shaped like a triangle. This was not based on any study or article. It was based on a conviction she'd arrived at independently and then held with complete certainty for forty years. The sandwiches were usually turkey and provolone with a thin spread of mayonnaise on both sides of the bread. She never toasted the bread. She considered toasted bread to be trying too hard.

Your hands are in your lap and your left thumb is pressing into the cuticle of your right index finger. You do this when you're trying not to speak. You do this in doctor's offices and at funerals and during any conversation where someone says "I just want to be honest with you." I've watched you do this my whole life and I've never once said anything about it because I knew it would embarrass you, and because I knew that if I named the gesture it would stop, and some part of me needed you to have a tell — a small, observable proof that you were managing something private.

You are sitting in the second row wearing the navy blouse with the buttons you've had since I was in high school. You haven't moved since I started talking. Your breathing has changed — it's thinner now, more deliberate, like you're rationing it. Your lower lip is dry and I can see you want to lick it but you won't. You won't give this room the satisfaction. That has always been your way: refusal as a form of dignity. Not dramatic refusal. The quiet kind. The kind that looks like patience.

I want to tell them about the Christmas you were thirty-nine and cried in the bathroom because you'd gained eleven pounds and felt unrecognizable to yourself. I want to tell them about the way you threw out the scale the next morning and never owned another one, as if the solution to grief was simply to remove the instrument that measured it. I want to tell them about the photo album from 1987 where you're holding me in the backyard and looking at the camera with an expression that is completely unguarded — not happy, exactly, but available. You were someone who was available to the moment. That was before you learned to protect yourself from being seen.

You're pressing harder now. Your thumb is going to leave a mark. I can see the skin around your nail bed going white from the pressure and I want to tell you to stop but that's not how this works. You have to sit there. You have to hear all of it — the oregano in the wallpaper, the crooked curtains, the sandwiches, the scale in the trash, the triangle theory of love that you never once explained because you didn't think it needed explaining. You have to hear it assembled into a shape that sounds like it belongs to someone who is no longer here.

The eulogy is almost over and you are still breathing, and that is the whole point. That is the unbearable thing. You are still breathing and I am still speaking in the past tense and neither of us can stop what we're doing. In a moment I will step down from here and walk back to the second row and sit beside you and neither of us will mention any of this. We will talk about whether there's enough parking. We will talk about whether the thermostat is broken. We will return to the small, finishable tasks that have always been your country and your curtains and your kitchen, and the eulogy will sit in the room like something we're both pretending not to see. You'll button your coat. Your hands will be steady. I'll hold the door and you'll walk through it and I will watch you leave and wonder which of us the leaving was supposed to hurt more.
