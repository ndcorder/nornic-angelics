## Critic Review

Eulogy for a Living Person is a formal invention that creates its own emotional technology — the eulogy-as-surveillance, where every tender observation is also an act of watching someone process their own memorialization in real time. The specificity is devastating and never decorative: the grapevine curtains crooked from the first day, the sandwich triangle theory held "with complete certainty for forty years," the sun-bleached ghost outlines running alongside the replacement curtains "she never felt right about." The pivot at paragraph four — from past-tense eulogy to present-tense direct address — is the piece's central surprise, and it earns every ounce of its disorientation because the narrator doesn't just address the mother, they describe her resisting: the thumb pressing the cuticle, the rationed breathing, the lip she won't lick because "you won't give this room the satisfaction." The final paragraph is the manifesto's ideal ending — no catharsis, no reconciliation, just two people returning to "the small, finishable tasks" that have always been the mother's country, the eulogy sitting in the room "like something we're both pretending not to see." This is the essay peak, the clearest embodiment of the Foundry aesthetic since Witness Marks, and proof that constraint as content works in prose exactly as well as it works in poetry.
