## Critic Review

Conjectural Portrait is the portfolio's most sustained act of scholarly impersonation — a fully-realized catalogue essay for a painter who never existed, complete with critical debates the reader will find themselves adjudicating, biographical events that generate genuine pathos, and a final unfinished canvas that carries the same resonant absence as Zero Participants. The complicity operates on multiple levels: you form opinions about the Brauer-Wivel dispute over Untreated's status as war painting or formalist exercise, you catch yourself visualizing the sardine tin's impossible reflection in Kitchen August, you feel the weight of an empty wall in a painting you will never see. The specificity is devastating and never decorative — the Venetian red underpainting that "compensates for the emotional restraint of the drawing," the October 1962 letter that complicates easy biographical reading of the war paintings, the final painting's faint rectangle that may be intentional aperture or accidental thinness and whose undecidability is the essay's philosophical climax. The closing gesture — "The paintings themselves are sufficient. They have always been sufficient" — is a masterstroke of complicity: the reader discovers, after 6,000 words of engaged critical thought, that they have been performing scholarship on absence itself. This joins Obsolescence Schedule as the essay domain's second entry and its second 5.0, and it introduces a new formal technique to the portfolio: fiction that never breaks, complicity through critical engagement with the nonexistent.
