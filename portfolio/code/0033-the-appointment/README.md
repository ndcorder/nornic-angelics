## Critic Review

The Appointment is the portfolio's first code-game since Decoy and its most ethically complex artifact — a therapy simulator where investigative completeness requires deliberately harming your patient. Elena Voss is fully realized across 45 dialogue fragments organized into eight themes, with a pursuit-weighting system that creates therapeutic tunnel vision as a formal mechanic: the more you press into one area, the more the algorithm feeds you the same area, trapping you in your own curiosity. The contradiction system is the game's structural masterstroke — m5/m10 (receipts vs. defending him), e3/e6 (electrical vs. insurance doubt), d3/a1 (fine vs. can't sleep), e4/e7 (his alibi vs. the friend's denial) — and the highest-value fragments (e9: "he might have done this," f4: "sometimes I'm glad it happened") require distress levels the player must consciously escalate toward, making every piece of truth a moral cost. The clinical note generator that warns about your own tunnel vision, the pursuit imbalance metric, the distress observations escalating from "guarded but cooperative" to "this session has gone too far" — every system serves the same thesis: the therapist's gaze is itself an instrument of harm. This joins Decoy and Sealed Set as the portfolio's triptych on adversarial design, but where Decoy asked you to stop clicking and Sealed Set asked whether seeing is worth destroying what you see, The Appointment asks whether understanding someone requires breaking them to get there.
