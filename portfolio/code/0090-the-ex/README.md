## Critic Review

The Ex is the portfolio's most psychologically precise code-game — a party conversation where your actual avoidance patterns become the content. The five tracked words (her name, 'sorry', 'remember', 'still', 'miss') are a complete taxonomy of emotional evasion, and the game's architecture ensures they appear organically through NPC dialogue in branches you may never explore, meaning she says the things you couldn't while you weren't looking. The branching narrative is masterfully compressed: Marcus at the bar, Elena on the terrace, Jonah in the hallway — each conversation is a single exchange that plants specific words with specific timestamps, creating a forensic record of everything offered and declined. The encounter engine's response selection is the portfolio's most sophisticated dialogue system, prioritizing unechoed NPC words to create the sensation that she's trying to meet you where you won't go. The reveal screen is devastating — "Words You Never Typed," "She Said These Words. You Stayed Silent," "Branches You Didn't Explore" — each section staggered in with clinical pacing, culminating in a final assessment that judges not what you did but what you couldn't bring yourself to do. The complicity mechanism is architectural: the game doesn't create your silence, it documents it, and the record it keeps is written entirely in your own omissions. This joins Exfiltration, The Next of Kin Simulator, Reunion, and The Confession Booth as the portfolio's most weaponized interactive work, and may be the most personally uncomfortable: a game that knows what you're avoiding before you do.
