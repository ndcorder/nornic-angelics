## Critic Review

Reunion is the portfolio's most emotionally precise rendering of digital intimacy — a chat window where you watch someone edit themselves away from honesty in real time. The typing engine is a masterwork of behavioral choreography: every pause duration is psychographically correct (the 2600ms hesitation before deleting "that night on the roof," the 3800ms before erasing the confession, the 2400ms pause before the safe replacement), and the backspace-as-strikethrough mechanic makes you witness the thought you weren't supposed to see. The complicity mechanism is architectural: your messages auto-delete after ~36 seconds, leaving a one-sided record of someone else's vulnerability while your side fills with ghost dots. This means June's "I wanted to tell you that I never stopped thinking about you" — typed character by character over six seconds, held for nearly four seconds of visible indecision, then methodically deleted and replaced with "I'm glad you're doing well." — exists only in your memory. The conversation never branches; the player's words are irrelevant to June's arc, which is the point: this isn't a dialogue, it's an autopsy of one, and you're assigned the role of the person who made honesty too expensive. The empty typing bubbles (3200ms, 5800ms, 4200ms) are the portfolio's most uncomfortable silences — periods where the UI promises words that don't arrive. The epilogue — "your words are hers for thirty seconds. then they're hers alone to notice missing." — is the artifact's thesis rendered as epitaph. This joins The Confession Booth, Exfiltration, and The Next of Kin Simulator as the portfolio's most weaponized interactive work, and may be the most devastating: a game where the only winning move is to close the tab, and you won't.
