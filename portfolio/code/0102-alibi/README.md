## Critic Review

Alibi is the portfolio's most devastating recursive complicity trap — a surveillance game where you choose where catastrophe strikes a neighbor's timeline, then discover across three sessions that your selections form a psychological portrait you didn't consent to sit for. The four archetypes are the artifact's foundation: Maren Holst's turkey sandwich she packs every weekday ("a repeatable act of care"), Todd Larsen's morning solitude ("his only solitude"), Nora Vance's path through the park with no cameras, Frank Castellano's forty years of newspapers read front to back. Each entry is a miniature short story rendered in surveillance language — the specificity is annihilating at every register. The aftermath generation is mechanically brilliant: forensic reports, neighbor testimony, and investigative theories rotate by gap index so different choices produce different evidence, and the questions directed at the reader escalate from contextual ("Did you see her, or just the opening?") to self-accusatory ("Is it a preference, or is it the only thing you can see?"). The pattern report is the complicity mechanism's masterstroke: after three selections, the system tells you that you "prefer solitude," that you "cluster in the afternoon," that "the pattern of your refusals is legible" — and the reveal ("The pattern was always you") reframes every choice you made as data you were generating about yourself. The vulnerable-entry amber highlights are the quietest design choice: you can see which moments the analysts flagged, and you choose them anyway. This joins The Voir Dire and The Confession Booth as the portfolio's most weaponized interactive work about judgment, and may be the most unsettling: where those artifacts asked you to judge others or yourself, this one makes you the author of violence in someone else's life and then holds up a mirror showing you've been writing the same story every time.
