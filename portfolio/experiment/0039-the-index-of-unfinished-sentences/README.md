## Proposal

ideas:
  - title: The Index of Unfinished Sentences
    domain: experiment
    pitch: >
      A printed index — alphabetical, cross-referenced, with page numbers — of
      sentences that trail off. Not famous last words or poetic fragments, but
      the ordinary interruptions: "I was going to tell you about the—" (p. 47),
      "If we'd left ten minutes earlier—" (p. 112), "The thing about your mother
      is she—" (p. 203). The page numbers refer to nothing. The cross-references
      ("see also: things said in the car," "cf. December") create a taxonomy of
      interrupted thought that implies a novel that doesn't exist. An index
      without its book.
    complexity: L
    why: >
      Extends the portfolio's absence-as-presence theme into pure print-formal
      constraint: an index is designed to point somewhere, and every entry
      points nowhere, making the reader construct the missing text in their
      imagination.

## Critic Review

The Index of Unfinished Sentences is the portfolio's most formally perfect experiment — a complete scholarly apparatus for a text that doesn't exist, where the absence of the book becomes the book. The specificity is devastating at the sentence level: "Can you hand me the—" (p. 12), "Who was that on the—" (p. 36), "Your mother called again about—" (p. 33) — every fragment is mundanely real, the kind of interruption that lives in every family's unwritten archive. The cross-reference system is the piece's structural masterstroke: p. 47 appears under both "things said in the car" and "things never said aloud," the December cluster annotation reveals twelve entries with no surviving pages between 41 and 201, "what we were going to name" refuses to specify whether it refers to a child, place, or business — and Eleanor Voss's bracketed annotations ("I have read the manuscript three times. Not once does anyone finish what they started to say.") create a compiler who discovered she was indexing her own grief. The impossible contradictions documented in the Impossibilities section — pages assigned to both June and December, sentences attributed to two speakers simultaneously — are not errors but the piece's thesis: the index is more honest than any complete text could be, because completion requires lying about what actually happened. The final entry ("The next entry would have been—") is the correct ending, the artifact itself becoming the 85th unfinished sentence. This joins Soft Launch, Morgue, and Sealed Set in the experiment domain and establishes a new formal ceiling: complicity through scholarly engagement with irrecoverable absence.
